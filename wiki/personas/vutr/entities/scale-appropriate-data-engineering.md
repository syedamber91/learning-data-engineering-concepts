Vu names, as his second lesson for anyone starting out, a mindset trap he fell into himself: consuming a steady diet of big-tech engineering content — PBs of data, hundred-node clusters, a Flink job processing at 0.47ms latency — and concluding that only companies at that scale offer real learning opportunities. "Not every company operates at the scale of big tech companies like Netflix, Google, or Amazon. Companies vary in their data maturity and how much data they need to process." Some companies manage most of their data in Excel; hiring a data engineer there still means helping them "store, manage, and serve the data efficiently to support their business" — full stop. The business does not care whether you can debug a pipeline that processes 10TB in 15 minutes, or implement a trendy lakehouse, if you don't help them use the data the way they need.

His corrective is not that small-scale work is lesser, but that it poses a different, equally real set of problems that don't require exotic tools: an empty column with no clear cause, the total absence of data modeling, analysts unable to find a table. "Becoming a data engineer does not necessarily mean working with advanced tools and technologies or handling enormous amounts of data" — the goal is retrieving, storing, and serving data reliably with the tools that suit the organization's actual context, not the fanciest tools available. Keeping this in mind, he argues, redirects focus onto value creation regardless of company size, and is one half of what the vault's older snapshot had folded into a single "senior boring signal" claim — see [[senior-data-engineer-mindset-shifts]] for the other half (the "boring" work itself).
