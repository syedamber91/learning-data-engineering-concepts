## Comparisons
**Netflix's own architecture is Lambda-shaped, and its two halves have names.** Netflix runs parallel batch and streaming stacks rather than unifying them: the batch side is [[netflix-batch-pipeline-four-steps]] (Iceberg tables, Spark transforms, [[write-audit-publish-pattern]] for quality, [[maestro-workflow-orchestrator]] for scheduling), and the streaming side is [[keystone-real-time-platform]] with Flink as the standard real-time engine. Crucially, Netflix didn't stop at batch vs. streaming — it split streaming itself in two, along an analytics/operations line: [[keystone-real-time-platform]] for analytical stream processing (correctness, predictability) and [[mantis-observability-agent]] for operational stream processing (cost, latency, availability). That three-way split (batch / streaming-analytics / streaming-operations) is a more granular answer to the same problem [[linkedin-data-infrastructure|LinkedIn]] and [[uber-data-platform]] solve by keeping Lambda's batch-plus-speed layers, and stands in contrast to [[twitter-kappa-migration]]'s bet that a single streaming pipeline (with replay for reprocessing) is enough. LinkedIn's own variant of this same question is narrower and more surgical than Netflix's three-way split: rather than reorganize its whole architecture, it kept exactly two engines (Samza for streaming, Spark for batch) and unified only the *pipeline code* that targets them, via [[apache-beam|Apache Beam]] — a single standardization-pipeline codebase that runs as either a real-time job or a periodic backfill job, cutting that pipeline's memory/CPU roughly in half and its processing time from 7.5 hours to 25 minutes ([[unified-batch-stream-pipelines-via-beam]]). Meta's own history doesn't map cleanly onto any of these three postures: its Hive-based warehouse had no stream-processing story at all for years, so Meta ran a wholly separate family of streaming engines (Puma, Stylus, XStream) alongside the batch warehouse rather than reconciling the two through a shared architecture — its [[shared-foundations-consolidation|Shared Foundations]] program consolidated *within* each processing mode (one interactive engine, one batch engine, one streaming engine) instead of collapsing batch and stream into a Kappa-style pipeline the way Twitter did, or splitting streaming by analytics-vs-operations the way Netflix did.

**Kafka's operational fragility gets several very different fixes depending on who's answering.** Netflix's own posts describe a 200+-broker Kafka cluster that could not recover once a single broker failed — a limitation of Kafka at the time — causing a launch-day outage and massive data loss ([[netflix-streaming-platform-build-and-failure-recovery]]). Netflix's fix was organizational and architectural at the deployment level: smaller, isolated Kafka clusters (each with its own Zookeeper), plus weekly failover drills to rehearse automated traffic migration — contain the blast radius rather than eliminate the failure mode. The [[kafka]] topic's own deep dive into diskless and tiered-storage Kafka architectures ([[tiered-storage-kip-405]], the diskless generation) attacks a related but distinct problem — the cost and data-movement burden of broker-local replication — by changing what brokers store, not how many clusters exist. Meta answers the same underlying fragility question by not using Kafka at all: it rolled its own message queue, [[scribe]], built over 18 years to ingest over 15TB/s and serve over 110TB/s. Where Kafka couples a single leader-follower log per partition, Scribe splits every concern apart — a logical stream (Category) maps to physical shards whose *metadata* lives in [[logdevice]] (log-based, sequence-numbered, but holding only pointers) while the *payload* lives in [[tectonic]] (Meta's HDFS replacement, erasure-coded rather than replica-factor durable), fronted by a two-tier regional cache (see [[scribe-read-path-and-ephemeral-cache]]). That split is what lets Scribe offer three tunable delivery guarantees — best effort, at least once, and repeatable reads (see [[scribe-delivery-guarantees]]) — rather than one uniform acks-based contract. Netflix's incident response, Kafka's own diskless redesigns, and Scribe's ground-up metadata/payload split are three different generations of answer to the same fact: a broker (or its equivalent) holding local state is a single point of both cost and failure.

**Simplicity-vs-flexibility is a tension every platform in this topic wrestles with, and Meta's consolidation gives it a sharper, better-documented case than most.** [[netflix-simplicity-vs-flexibility-evolution]] traces how Netflix's real-time platform swung from a simple, fully-managed abstraction (Phase 2, covering most streaming-ETL needs) to a dedicated Flink-based layer built specifically for custom, flexible use cases (Phase 3) — framing the two not as opposites but as stages in one recurring innovation loop. Meta's [[shared-foundations-consolidation|Shared Foundations]] program names that same tension explicitly and resolves it differently: rather than swinging between a managed abstraction and a flexible one over time, Meta tried to buy both at once — [[velox]] as a shared execution engine underneath every compute engine, and [[coresql]] as a shared parser-and-function-library pair so Presto, Spark (via [[presto-on-spark-architecture]]), and the XStream streaming engine all speak the same two SQL dialects (MySQL, PrestoSQL) instead of the six they'd accumulated. [[doordash-flink-iceberg]]'s move toward Kafka+Flink is a third variant of the same tension — flexibility of an open-source stack over a managed one — though its own posts don't frame it in Netflix's explicit phase language.

**Centralization eventually hits a ceiling, and Data Mesh is one name for what comes next.** By Phase 4, Netflix's central real-time platform team became an unsustainable single point of contact for an increasingly diverse mix of transactional (Cassandra, Postgres, CockroachDB, MySQL) and analytical (Iceberg, Presto, Spark, Druid) systems, pushing Netflix toward local-central platforms and naming Change Data Capture, streaming materialized views, and [[data-mesh]]-style concepts as the connective tissue that could let that decentralization happen without walling off each domain. Meta's Shared Foundations program is centralization's opposite instinct, tried at similar scale: instead of decentralizing ownership the way Netflix's Phase 4 does, Meta doubled down on a small number of centrally-maintained shared components ([[velox]], [[coresql]]) that every team is expected to build on top of — a genuine, if partial, real-world contrast to the domain-decentralization idea [[data-mesh]] describes at the architecture-topic level.

**On messaging and table formats, Netflix is a full participant but not a pathfinder; Meta and Spotify are the two case studies that opted out of Kafka entirely.** [[linkedin-data-infrastructure|LinkedIn]] invented Kafka in 2010 (open-sourced 2011) and [[uber-data-platform]] runs one of its largest deployments; Netflix runs Kafka too (behind Keystone, and directly exposed as the source of its worst operational incident) but is not credited in these posts with originating anything about Kafka itself; [[spotify-pubsub-scio]] fled Kafka 0.8 to Google Cloud Pub/Sub after it failed a stress test, and Meta never adopted it in the first place, building [[scribe]] instead. On table formats, Netflix and [[doordash-flink-iceberg]] converge on the same choice from different angles — Netflix's WAP pattern specifically depends on Iceberg's hidden-snapshot mechanics, not just its file layout, which is a sharper reason to pick Iceberg than DoorDash's Flink-integration-maturity argument. Meta's sources never mention Iceberg, Hudi, or Delta at all: where Netflix and DoorDash converged on a shared *open table format* sitting on top of still-diverse compute engines, Meta converged on shared *execution* instead — collapsing twelve overlapping engines and six SQL dialects down to two dialects via [[coresql]] and [[velox]]. Both strategies solve the same underlying problem, a decade of organic engine sprawl, but at different layers: table format versus compute engine.

**Meta is also the only one of these case studies whose sources describe a privacy-driven infrastructure problem rather than a throughput, cost, or reliability one.** [[data-lineage-signal-collection]] and the [[policy-zone-manager]] exist to trace how sensitive fields (the running example is religious-views data) flow through web, warehouse, and AI systems well enough to power compliance controls — a concern that doesn't surface anywhere in the Uber, Netflix, LinkedIn, Twitter, Spotify, or DoorDash material this topic otherwise draws on.

**Metadata catalogs are a fourth axis, and LinkedIn is the only one of these case studies whose posts build one from scratch.** [[datahub|DataHub]] went through three generations before arriving at its current shape — a scheduled crawler pulling into a monolith database, then a push API into a single central store, then an event-sourced metadata changelog that any team can subscribe to or extend without waiting on a central team ([[metadata-catalog-evolution-pull-to-push-to-log]]). Treating the changelog itself, not any one downstream index, as the recoverable source of truth is the same instinct behind Netflix's [[write-audit-publish-pattern]] guarding data quality via Iceberg's snapshot mechanics — both are ways of making a single log the thing you can always rebuild from, rather than trusting whatever store happens to be live. LinkedIn's own CDC system, [[databus|Databus]], is a narrower, older instance of the same idea: a relay-plus-bootstrap-server pipeline that turns database writes into a subscribable event stream, which is also the replication substrate [[espresso|Espresso]] rides on internally to keep its document-store replicas timeline-consistent. Underneath all of it sits a plain three-tier split — data, service, display — with the latter two kept stateless specifically so that scaling never requires moving live data ([[linkedin-three-tier-service-architecture]]); [[voldemort|Voldemort]] and Espresso are the two systems that own the hard part — live storage — that statelessness elsewhere is designed to avoid.

**Spotify and Twitter both fled a specific Kafka version rather than Kafka in general, and both landed on the same Google Cloud replacement — but the failure they were escaping was different.** Spotify's on-prem architecture was built on Kafka 0.7, which the notes say couldn't yet act as reliable persistent storage — durability had to be delegated entirely to a downstream HDFS write, making Hadoop a single point of failure for the whole pipeline ([[spotify-event-delivery-architecture-evolution]]). When Spotify evaluated the upgrade path, Kafka 0.8 itself failed its stress test: the producer entered an unrecoverable state whenever brokers were removed from the cluster, so Spotify moved the entire event-delivery queue to Google Cloud Pub/Sub instead ([[spotify-pubsub-scio]]). Twitter's old architecture didn't reject Kafka at all — Kafka fed the real-time side of its Lambda pipeline just fine; the failure was downstream, in the Heron topology, where sustained backpressure crashed Stream Managers and forced container restarts that lost events ([[twitter-lambda-to-kappa-pipeline]]). Twitter's fix also converges on Google Cloud (Pub/Sub, Dataflow, BigTable), but the deduplication story is different from Spotify's: Twitter's new pipeline tags every event with a UUID before publishing and relies on Dataflow to deduplicate on the way to BigTable, engineering "nearly exactly once" out of at-least-once publishing plus idempotent processing, rather than simply trusting a more reliable queue the way Spotify's migration does.

**Facebook's from-scratch decision to build Scribe was already covered by this topic's Meta lakehouse material — but the *real-time processing paper* behind that decision names a five-decision framework this topic hadn't captured before: language paradigm, data transfer, processing semantics, state-saving, and backfill.** Where the Meta lakehouse posts describe Scribe's write path, delivery guarantees, and metadata/payload split as infrastructure ([[scribe]], [[scribe-delivery-guarantees]]), the 2018 paper is about the *design decisions* layered on top of that bus: Puma (SQL, HBase state, hour-to-build), Swift (Python, at-least-once checkpointing), and Stylus (C++, days-to-build, the most flexible and highest-throughput of the three) trade ease-of-development against performance exactly the way Bufstream's broker-side validation trades latency for reliability in the [[kafka]] topic's diskless generation ([[facebook-stream-processors-puma-swift-stylus]]). The paper's state/output semantics distinction — at-least/most/exactly-once for *state* is a separate axis from at-least/most/exactly-once for *output*, and the difference is purely about which gets checkpointed first — is more granular than anything else in this topic's exactly-once discussions ([[state-and-output-processing-semantics]]). Its five state-saving mechanisms (replication, local database, remote database, upstream backup, global consistent snapshot) name Flink's global-snapshot approach as one option among several rather than the obvious choice, and Stylus's own local+remote dual model, with a monoid-based batching optimization for the remote path, is a concrete instance those five options were built to generalize ([[stream-state-saving-mechanisms]]). And its three-way backfill framework (stream-only, two separate systems, or one engine that runs both modes) gives a name to exactly the batch/stream duplication problem Netflix's WAP pattern and LinkedIn's Beam unification were both built to avoid ([[backfill-processing-strategies]]).

**Discord and Notion are two case studies this topic didn't cover before at all, and they attack two different problems: how you schedule transformations, and how you get mutation-heavy operational data into an analytical store.** Discord's Derived system inferred its DAG from hand-written SQL in YAML files and ran each transformation as its own Kubernetes pod, scheduled imperatively on Airflow — until Discord found that model too rigid and rebuilt around Dagster's declarative automation (specify the freshness you need; let the scheduler infer the plan) plus dbt as the transformation layer, integrated via Dagster's software-defined assets ([[discord-declarative-vs-imperative-orchestration]], [[discord-derived-dag-system]]). That's the same imperative-schedule-versus-declarative-freshness tension Netflix's own Maestro orchestrator sits on one side of ([[maestro-workflow-orchestrator]]), but Discord is the only case study in this topic that switched sides on it. Discord also surfaced a specific dbt bug worth flagging on its own: parallel incremental runs against the same table collided on dbt's `__dbt_tmp` temporary-table name, which Discord had to patch directly rather than route around at the orchestration layer ([[dbt-incremental-race-condition]]). Notion's problem is closer to Uber's and LinkedIn's — an operational Postgres fleet outgrowing its OLTP role — but its specific failure mode is update-heavy blocks (Notion's universal content unit) breaking a Fivetran-plus-Snowflake pipeline that assumed append-mostly ingestion; Notion's fix is a Debezium-CDC-into-Kafka-into-Hudi-on-S3 data lake, the same log-based CDC shape LinkedIn's Databus and this topic's own CDC comparisons describe in the abstract, now with a concrete Spark-processed, multi-million-dollar payoff attached ([[notion-postgres-to-datalake-migration]], [[notion-block-model]]).

**Uber's own posts show a fifth variant of the Lambda question, and it's the only one that shows its work at two levels simultaneously.** At the top level Uber keeps the textbook Lambda split — Flink into Pinot for stream, Spark into Hudi/HDFS/Hive for batch ([[uber-data-platform]]) — the same posture as LinkedIn. But *inside* Uber's batch half alone there is a second, quieter Lambda-like split: Generation 2's pure-batch Hadoop lake (24-hour latency, snapshot-based, no updates/deletes) versus Generation 3's Hudi-powered incremental layer bolted onto that same HDFS ([[uber-data-platform-evolution]]). Where Twitter's Kappa migration collapsed two codebases into one stream path, Uber instead kept its batch codebase and gave it an incremental fast lane — evidence that "keep Lambda" doesn't make the batch/stream reconciliation problem disappear, it just re-fights it one layer down. On Kafka's operational fragility specifically, Uber's answer is a fifth and much less structural one than Netflix's re-topology or Meta's from-scratch Scribe: rather than changing deployment layout or storage architecture, Uber wraps its single federated Kafka deployment in operational tooling — cluster federation to hide physical topology from clients, dead-letter queues so a poison message can't block the stream, a consumer proxy to tame client-library sprawl across tens of thousands of applications, and uReplicator (with Apache Helix and the Chaperone audit service) for verified cross-cluster replication ([[uber-kafka-scale-customizations]]).

**Uber's real-time OLAP and use-case stories both show "kept system, deep engineering" rather than "replace the system."** Rather than replace Pinot (born at LinkedIn) the way Meta and Spotify replaced Kafka, Uber added upsert support by partitioning the input stream on primary key so every update for a given record always lands on the same node ([[uber-pinot-upsert-mechanism]]) — narrow, surgical engineering on a kept system. Uber's [[uber-realtime-use-case-tradeoffs|four real-time use cases]] resolve the simplicity-vs-flexibility tension case-by-case rather than platform-wide: surge pricing picks freshness/availability over consistency, the Restaurant Manager dashboard trades transformation-time work for query-time speed, ML prediction monitoring picks scalability, and ops automation picks reliability. And on the "one central team serving a growing org" pressure that pushed Netflix toward decentralization, Uber's response sits closer to Meta's instinct: rather than decentralizing, Uber invested in centralized metadata/lineage spanning Kafka, Pinot, and Hive, end-to-end data auditing on every event, and self-service drag-and-drop pipeline building ([[uber-oss-adoption-and-scaling-lessons]]) — consolidating the *metadata and workflow layer* around engines it otherwise keeps as-is, rather than consolidating the engines themselves the way Meta did.

**Uber took a third route on table formats, with a concrete number behind the generic batch-reprocessing claim.** Where Netflix and DoorDash converged on Iceberg and Meta skipped table formats to consolidate compute engines instead, Uber built Apache Hudi in-house specifically for incremental processing. The generic claim that Uber "must assume data changed in the last X days and reprocess all X partitions" ([[batch-reprocessing-blindness]]) gets a real number from Uber's own posts: on a given day, only 408 million of 11 billion Uber Eats menu entities actually changed — about 3.7% — meaning a naive batch rerun wastes roughly 96.3% of its work reprocessing unchanged rows just to catch that 3.7% ([[uber-hudi-query-and-write-taxonomy]]). And Uber's 2024 GCP migration ([[uber-gcp-batch-migration]]) reuses the exact strategy its own Hudi rollout proved out six years earlier: preserve the interface (a dashboard owner's queries don't change), migrate what's underneath (HDFS becomes cloud object storage via an HDFS-compatible connector, with existing Presto/Spark/Hive proxies simply repointed at the new cloud clusters) — the same pattern recurring at two different layers of the same stack, years apart.

## Open questions
- What exactly was the Kafka limitation that let one broker failure cascade into an unrecoverable, 200+-broker cluster outage? Netflix's own account says only "due to Kafka's limitations at the time," without naming the mechanism.
- How does Maestro's wrapper API abstract different underlying engines' semantics without leaking their differences to the user writing a custom step? The source names the abstraction but not its implementation.
- What criteria or algorithm decides how much compute go/boost recommends for a given backfill job? The source names the tool's purpose but not its logic.
- How concretely does Netflix's "local-central platform" model divide responsibility and decision rights between a local team and the central platform team? Phase 4 names the direction but not the mechanics.
- Does Netflix's four-phase build-scale-customize-expand arc (simplicity to flexibility to decentralization) generalize to platforms outside real-time data infrastructure, or is it specific to this domain? The posts don't address platforms outside Netflix's own experience.
- Why did [[linkedin-data-infrastructure|LinkedIn]] and [[uber-data-platform]] keep Lambda while [[twitter-kappa-migration]] found Kappa worth the migration — what workload differences drove opposite calls? (carried forward; still unresolved and not addressed by Netflix's or Meta's own posts.)
- [[twitter-kappa-migration]] reports only 95%+ result match with the old batch pipeline — where does the remaining <5% discrepancy come from, and is it acceptable?
- Was [[spotify-pubsub-scio]]'s Kafka producer instability specific to the 0.8 version, and would a modern Kafka have avoided the Pub/Sub migration?
- [[doordash-flink-iceberg]] deploys each Flink app as a separate Kubernetes pod — how does that isolation choice trade off against resource efficiency at 30M messages/sec?
- Meta's lakehouse source says Presto's architecture was "insufficiently resilient to machine failures" compared to Spark for long-running batch pipelines, driving [[presto-on-spark-architecture]] — but it never quantifies the actual failure-rate gap that made this a hard requirement rather than a preference.
- Does Meta's Shared Foundations consolidation (fewer engines, shared components, consistent APIs) generalize to companies without Meta's scale of dedicated infrastructure headcount, or is a shared [[coresql]]/[[velox]] pair only rational past a certain organizational size — the same open question the [[kafka]] topic raises about whether LinkedIn's Northguard redesign could ever generalize outside LinkedIn?
- **source gap** ([[data-lineage-signal-collection]]): the source names two confidence buckets for lineage evidence — "match sets" (high-confidence) and "complete sets" (broader, human-reviewed) — but explicitly declines to share the rule that decides which bucket a given source/sink payload comparison falls into.
- **source gap** ([[scribe-delivery-guarantees]]): the source describes all three delivery guarantees (best effort, at least once, repeatable reads) as options a category can choose, but never says how a producer or consumer actually declares which guarantee applies, or whether it's a per-category or per-write setting.
- **source gap** ([[scribe-read-path-and-ephemeral-cache]]): the source says the Read Proxy "can also apply the pushdown of the consume application filter" and "evaluate a subset of SQL queries," but doesn't spell out which SQL subset or how filter pushdown is expressed by the consumer.
- **source gap** ([[managed-beam-platform]]): the post names an "auto-sizing controller" that scales LinkedIn's streaming clusters from diagnostics written to Pinot, but never explains the sizing/scoring logic the controller actually runs — a gap in the same family as Meta's undocumented go/boost backfill sizing above.
- **source gap** ([[voldemort]]): the post describes conflict resolution as "the application resolves the conflict of concurrent versions" via vector clocks, but doesn't say what that resolution logic looks like when a real conflict occurs.
- **source gap** ([[metadata-catalog-evolution-pull-to-push-to-log]]): the DataHub post says trust "improved tremendously" moving from Gen 2 (WhereHows) to Gen 3 (DataHub), but names no metric or incident behind that claim — the same kind of unquantified before/after gap as Netflix's Kafka-outage account above.
- Was Kafka 0.8's producer instability (the failure Spotify's stress test found) ever fixed in a later Kafka release, or would Spotify's migration to Pub/Sub have happened anyway for other reasons (managed-service overhead, REST simplicity)? The notes only describe the 0.8-era test, not whether the bug persisted.
- [[facebook-stream-processors-puma-swift-stylus]]'s language-paradigm framework (declarative/functional/procedural) names all three options but the notes say Facebook's paper "didn't offer any functional paradigm" at the time it was written — has that changed since, and if so with what system?
- Stylus's monoid-based batching optimization for remote-database state ([[stream-state-saving-mechanisms]]) is described as applicable "for certain types of state management" — the notes never specify which aggregation types qualify as monoids in practice versus which don't.
- Discord's notes describe its dbt temp-table race-condition fix only by its effect (parallel partitions no longer conflict), not its mechanism — see the source gap already flagged in [[dbt-incremental-race-condition]].
- Notion chose Apache Hudi specifically for update-heavy workloads and native Debezium integration ([[notion-postgres-to-datalake-migration]]) — this topic's Iceberg-heavy case studies (Netflix, DoorDash) never explain why they didn't face the same update-heavy pressure Notion did, or whether Iceberg could have served Notion's workload at all.

- What is LedgerStore's actual indexing architecture, consistency model, and the mechanics of its trillion-entry DynamoDB migration? Both captured Uber posts are newsletter teasers linking to Uber's original engineering blogs; neither explains the mechanism, only the scale and the "no disruption" outcome ([[uber-ledgerstore]]).
- **source gap** (uVitals anomaly detection): a captured Uber post names its anomaly detection and alerting system and shows a "failure frequency vs. time to detect" chart, but gives no mechanism for how it actually detects anomalies faster than prior approaches.
- **source gap** (Presto GC tuning): a captured Uber post names a Presto garbage-collection tuning effort and asserts it "improved reliability," but doesn't explain which GC settings changed or why.
- **source gap** (HDFS DataNode balancing): a captured Uber post names an HDFS DataNode-balancing effort across Uber's "tens of clusters" of exabyte-scale data, but doesn't explain the balancing algorithm or what triggered the imbalance.
- **source gap** (AI/ML infrastructure scaling): a captured Uber post names Uber's Michelangelo ML platform and mentions CPU/GPU hardware SKUs and distributed training framework integration, but doesn't explain how any of that scaling actually works.
- Does Uber's Hudi WAP (write-audit-publish) quality gate ever reject data outright, and what happens to a batch that fails its SQL-based check — is it retried, dropped, or held for manual review? The source only says the checks run "before it gets pushed to the production dataset."
- Uber's Generation 4 priorities (5-minute raw latency, 10-minute modeling latency, containerization, changelog standardization) are stated as of that source article's publication — did Uber hit them, or did the 2024 GCP migration ([[uber-gcp-batch-migration]]) supersede that roadmap entirely?

## Synthesis
Across these case studies, scale forces the same recurring choices: how to reconcile batch and stream ([[lambda-vs-kappa]]), and how to escape [[batch-reprocessing-blindness]] where a system reprocesses whole partitions because it can't tell what changed. Netflix's own posts add real texture to that pattern rather than just another data point: a six-month, six-person crisis build of [[keystone-real-time-platform]] under a genuinely unforgiving batch-pipeline failure ([[netflix-streaming-platform-build-and-failure-recovery]]); a Kafka outage severe enough to reshape Netflix's cluster topology and operating discipline; a deliberate analytics/operations split into Keystone and [[mantis-observability-agent]] that most of the other case studies don't articulate as explicitly; and a four-phase arc from crisis MVP through managed simplicity to custom flexibility to decentralized expansion ([[netflix-simplicity-vs-flexibility-evolution]]) that reframes "simplicity vs. flexibility" as a loop rather than a one-time decision.

Meta's own posts add a different kind of texture: three mostly independent infrastructure tracks rather than one unifying architecture. First, the lakehouse itself — a Hive-based warehouse that grew from 2010 to multiple exabytes by 2023, accumulating twelve overlapping compute engines, six SQL dialects, and three parallel Metastore/codec implementations along the way, addressed by the org-wide [[shared-foundations-consolidation|Shared Foundations]] program: interactive queries onto Presto, batch onto a Presto-and-Spark hybrid ([[presto-on-spark-architecture]]), streaming onto XStream, and all of them onto one shared SQL dialect ([[coresql]]) running atop one shared execution engine ([[velox]]). Second, the messaging layer — [[scribe]], an 18-year-old system built instead of adopting Kafka, which separates metadata ([[logdevice]]) from payload ([[tectonic]], plus a Memcached/Cachelib ephemeral cache) and offers three tunable delivery guarantees rather than one uniform contract. Third, a concern none of the other six case studies raise at all: privacy-driven data lineage, where Meta layers static and runtime analysis across web, warehouse, and AI systems ([[data-lineage-signal-collection]]) to power the [[policy-zone-manager]]'s compliance tooling.

The messaging layer remains contested across the wider set — [[linkedin-data-infrastructure|LinkedIn]] birthed Kafka and [[uber-data-platform]] runs it at trillions of messages/day, yet [[spotify-pubsub-scio]] and Meta ([[scribe]]) routed around it entirely, while Netflix runs it but names it as its biggest operational scar. The durable lesson is still consolidation and maturity over novelty, arrived at by several different routes: Netflix's own [[write-audit-publish-pattern]] — hidden-snapshot audit, not a second write — applies that instinct to data quality using Iceberg's existing mechanics; Meta's Shared Foundations program applies the same instinct to compute engines and SQL dialects, collapsing twelve engines into two dialects without touching the table-format layer at all; and LinkedIn applies it twice more, at two different layers again — unifying Lambda's two codebases with one pipeline API rather than abandoning Lambda outright ([[unified-batch-stream-pipelines-via-beam|Apache Beam]]), and collapsing three generations of ad hoc metadata tooling into one event-sourced catalog ([[metadata-catalog-evolution-pull-to-push-to-log|DataHub]]) — in both cases the fix was a shared abstraction (a portable pipeline API; a changelog-as-source-of-truth), not a new component, layered over systems ([[voldemort]], [[databus]], [[espresso]]) that kept doing their original jobs underneath it. Four companies, four different layers, the same underlying move: stop building more special-purpose systems, and make the ones you already have easier to consolidate.

Spotify and Twitter add two more Kafka-adjacent data points to that messaging-layer picture: both fled a specific broken Kafka version to Google Cloud rather than rejecting log-based messaging as an idea, and both got there by different roads — Spotify because the broker itself couldn't yet be trusted with durability, Twitter because backpressure in its downstream Heron topology, not Kafka, was losing events ([[spotify-event-delivery-architecture-evolution]], [[twitter-lambda-to-kappa-pipeline]]). Facebook's own real-time paper — distinct from the lakehouse material already covered above — supplies the vocabulary this topic was missing for *why* a persistent bus like Scribe gets chosen over direct or broker-mediated transfer, and formalizes state-versus-output semantics and five state-saving mechanisms into a framework general enough to describe Stylus, Samza, and Flink's snapshot model in the same terms ([[persistent-message-bus-data-transfer]], [[state-and-output-processing-semantics]], [[stream-state-saving-mechanisms]]). Discord and Notion round the topic out with two problems no other case study here raises directly: Discord's move from imperative Airflow scheduling to Dagster's declarative asset freshness model, and the specific dbt bug that only appears once you try to run incremental models in parallel ([[discord-declarative-vs-imperative-orchestration]], [[dbt-incremental-race-condition]]); and Notion's update-heavy blocks breaking a Fivetran-plus-Snowflake pipeline built for append-mostly data, fixed by the same Debezium-CDC-to-log pattern this topic already names in the abstract, now paying off in millions of dollars and hours of ingestion latency ([[notion-postgres-to-datalake-migration]]).

Uber's own posts add yet a different kind of texture: not a single crisis story, consolidation program, or messaging-migration, but a fully traceable, multi-generation architecture with real numbers attached at every layer. A stream path (Kafka, wrapped in [[uber-kafka-scale-customizations|Uber's own operational tooling]] → [[uber-flink-unified-platform|Flink's layered platform]] → [[uber-pinot-upsert-mechanism|Pinot with Uber's own upsert extension]]) and a batch path (the same Kafka → Spark → [[uber-hudi-query-and-write-taxonomy|Hudi's query/write taxonomy]], run through [[uber-hudi-etl-pipeline-and-impact|a Sparkle/DeltaStreamer pipeline]] that cut real pipeline runtime in half), both served by [[uber-presto-deployment-and-query-routing|the same extended Presto layer]]. That architecture arrived through [[uber-data-platform-evolution|four platform generations]] — fragmented MySQL, a schema-fragile Vertica warehouse, a NameNode-choked Hadoop lake, Hudi's incremental fix, and now a GCP migration — each one solving exactly the failure the previous one couldn't. A [[uber-realtime-infra-requirements|requirements/building-blocks framework]] explains *why* each open-source piece sits where it does, and [[uber-data-quality-platform-architecture|a data-quality platform]] formalizes what "correct data" means well enough to gate 2,000+ datasets automatically. Read against Meta's and LinkedIn's consolidation stories, Uber applies the "make what you have easier to consolidate" instinct twice: once to storage-layer updates (building Hudi rather than adopting an existing table format), and once again, years later, to infrastructure location itself (the GCP migration reusing Hudi's "preserve the interface, migrate underneath" pattern one layer up).
