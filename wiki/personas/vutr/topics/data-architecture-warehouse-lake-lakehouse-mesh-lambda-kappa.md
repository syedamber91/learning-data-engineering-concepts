## Comparisons
**Centralized vs. decentralized, and everything else is a pattern layered on top.** Vu's own top-level split is architecture-vs-architecture: the centralized line ([[data-warehouse]] → [[data-lake]] → [[lakehouse]]) versus the decentralized [[data-mesh]]. Everything else he covers gets demoted, deliberately, to a smaller category — see [[architecture-vs-pattern]]: [[medallion-architecture]], [[lambda-architecture]], and [[kappa-architecture]] are patterns ("a reusable solution to a specific problem"), Data Fabric is mostly marketing dressed up as an architecture, and the Modern Data Stack is a philosophy, not a blueprint. This taxonomy matters because it's the frame Vu uses to resist buzzword inflation — a new term earns "architecture" status only if it changes the ingestion-to-serving blueprint, not just one layer of it.

**Warehouse vs. lake vs. lakehouse: the same story, told three times.** Across three of Vu's posts (`do-we-need-the-lakehouse-architecture`, `the-data-lake-warehouse-and-lakehouse`, `data-architecture-101`) the arc repeats with the same beats: the [[data-warehouse]] centralizes structured data but chokes on unstructured formats and hardware-bound on-prem scaling; the [[data-lake]] (Hadoop/HDFS-era, later cloud object storage) accepts anything schema-on-read but degrades into a "data swamp" without ACID, DML, discovery, or quality controls; the two get stitched into a two-tier lake-plus-warehouse pattern that dominates for over a decade but costs reliability (ETL glue), freshness (warehouse always stale versus the lake), ML support (exports and ODBC/JDBC instead of native reads), and money (storage paid twice); and the [[lakehouse]] tries to end the split by adding a real transactional metadata layer — see [[lakehouse-metadata-layer-as-translator]] — directly on top of cheap object storage.

**The lakehouse's own unresolved tension: BigQuery/Snowflake meet the letter, break the spirit.** Vu returns to this exact point in three separate posts, which signals it's the crux of his skepticism about the term: BigQuery and Snowflake already decouple compute (stateless query engines) from storage (Colossus / S3-backed) behind a metadata/translation layer — technically satisfying the lakehouse definition — but the vendor controls that storage layer and dictates which engines can attach, which is precisely what the lakehouse manifesto promised to end. He notes the gap is closing from both sides (BigQuery/Snowflake increasingly query externally-owned Iceberg/Delta data; open formats increasingly support BigQuery/Snowflake as just one of several possible engines), but doesn't call the tension resolved.

**Six questions replace one vendor.** "The 6 questions you must answer when building a Lakehouse from scratch" — see [[lakehouse-build-decision-framework]] — is Vu's inventory of everything BigQuery/Snowflake quietly do for you that a self-built lakehouse forces you to decide yourself: table format(s), query engine(s), physical storage layout, metadata-file management, access control, and developer/user experience. His own laptop-scale build (MinIO + [[project-nessie]] + [[trino]] + dbt + Airflow) is the concrete, hands-on version of exactly these six decisions made small enough to fit on one machine — three schemas (landing/staging/curated) is his own instance of the Medallion-style layering pattern, built from scratch rather than inherited from a platform.

**Two ways to make the "own everything" cost disappear.** Vu profiles two systems that each remove a different piece of the self-build burden without becoming a full BigQuery/Snowflake-style vendor lock-in. [[bauplan]] removes infrastructure from the *compute* side: stateless, per-run-scaled FaaS execution over Iceberg + Nessie storage, with Arrow-based data exchange (shared memory/Arrow Flight) replacing the S3-only intermediate-data hop that slows down generic FaaS data pipelines. [[project-nessie]] removes a specific *storage* limitation: Iceberg alone only guarantees atomicity at the table level, and Nessie adds a git-like versioned catalog on top specifically to make cross-table commits atomic — the same git-like property Vu says drew him to choose it for his own Trino build, independent of Bauplan.

**Lambda vs. Kappa: one trade-off, told in the same terms as everything else.** [[lambda-architecture]] runs batch (accurate, delayed) and stream (fast, less accurate) paths in parallel and reconciles them for users, at the cost of two codebases; [[kappa-architecture]] collapses both into one stream (replaying history through the same code, e.g. re-consuming Kafka from an earlier offset), trading the dual-codebase cost for a requirement of real stream-system expertise (Kafka, Flink, Spark micro-batching). Vu classifies both as patterns for data processing and serving, not full architectures — the same demotion he applies to Medallion.

## Open questions
- Vu says BigQuery/Snowflake violate the lakehouse manifesto's *spirit* by controlling the storage layer, while meeting its technical letter — but he never settles whether "you must control your own storage" is actually part of the definition or just an ideal he personally holds. As the vendor/open gap narrows further (per his own note that vendors now let you query externally-owned Iceberg/Delta data), does the distinction keep meaning anything?
- Does Kappa fully retire Lambda in practice, or does the stream-system expertise Kappa demands (Vu names it as the trade for Kappa's single codebase) keep Lambda alive for teams that don't have that expertise? The posts state the trade-off but don't report which one actually wins in practice.
- Data Mesh's blocker is named as a "mindset change" plus added hiring cost, but the posts never describe what a *successful* transition actually looks like, or point to a company that pulled it off — an open gap Vu himself doesn't close.
- The 6-questions framework prescribes an MVP-and-benchmark evaluation for table format and query engine choices, but doesn't say how to weigh those criteria against each other when they conflict (e.g. a format that wins on schema evolution but loses on concurrency) — the posts describe the inputs to the decision, not how to resolve trade-offs between them.
- **source gap** ([[lakehouse-metadata-layer-as-translator]]): the posts describe metadata-file proliferation as a real bottleneck and DuckLake's database-backed alternative, but don't explain how a database-backed metadata store like DuckLake's actually avoids the same problem, or what its own trade-offs are.
- **source gap** ([[bauplan]]): the posts describe Bauplan's per-run instance scaling (10GB run vs 100GB run, new instances each time) but not how instance sizing is actually requested or provisioned.
- **source gap** ([[trino]]): the posts show Trino's coordinator/worker split and connector-based catalogs but not how the coordinator actually distributes work across workers or recovers from a worker failure mid-query.

## Synthesis
Vu's data-architecture writing is best read as one long argument against taking a vendor's or a blog's terminology at face value. The [[data-warehouse]] → [[data-lake]] → [[lakehouse]] arc is a single reconciliation story told three separate times, always ending at the same place: a transactional metadata layer ([[lakehouse-metadata-layer-as-translator]]) bolted onto cheap object storage, tuned for actual query speed with caching, statistics, and physical layout tricks ([[lakehouse-query-performance-techniques]]). But Vu refuses to let "lakehouse" become a synonym for "good architecture" — BigQuery and Snowflake meet its letter while, in his view, betraying its spirit by keeping the storage layer proprietary, and he holds every other term in the space (Medallion, Data Fabric, Modern Data Stack, Lambda, Kappa) to the same test of whether it's actually an architecture or just a pattern, philosophy, or marketing label — see [[architecture-vs-pattern]]. When he actually walks the reader through building a lakehouse from zero — the six-question framework ([[lakehouse-build-decision-framework]]), his own MinIO/Nessie/Trino/dbt/Airflow laptop build, and his profile of Bauplan as a way to operate a lakehouse with none of that infrastructure — the throughline is [[every-decision-has-a-tradeoff]]: none of this is worth doing unless the organization's actual requirements demand it, and for most teams, handing pieces of the stack to a vendor is the correct trade, not a failure of ambition.
