## Comparisons
**Two papers, one architecture, read six years apart.** Vu's three posts aren't independent takes — they're the same system described by a 2016 paper (Snowflake's original design: shared-nothing-vs-shared-disk split, storage pool, VW as "the muscle," Cloud Services as "the brain," Snapshot Isolation, semi-structured data, time travel) and a 2020 paper that zooms into exactly one part the first paper glossed over: the ephemeral storage system, locality-aware scheduling, VW elasticity, and multi-tenancy economics. The 2024 synthesis post then re-reads both together. The interesting thing the sequence reveals is what survived six years unchanged versus what didn't: the storage-compute split, the PAX-like proprietary file format, and vectorized push-based execution appear in both papers as settled design; the ephemeral-storage cache and scheduling mechanics, by contrast, only get a full treatment once the 2020 paper exists specifically to work through them, and Snowflake's own "future direction" callouts in that paper (fine-grained cache analysis, shared multi-tenant ephemeral storage, intra-query elasticity) are framed as still-open problems even in the paper that most deeply covers them.

**Consistent hashing shows up twice, for two different jobs.** [[ephemeral-storage-tiering-and-persistent-caching]] uses consistent hashing to decide which node's local disk *caches* a given persistent file; [[locality-aware-scheduling-and-work-stealing]] then piggybacks on that same hash assignment to decide which node *runs* the task touching that file. They're presented as one mechanism with two consumers, not two separate systems — which is exactly why a scheduling hotspot (work stealing) and a cache-locality problem (lazy rehashing) both trace back to the same underlying hash function, and why Snowflake's stated future work for one ("private per-tenant address spaces" in [[virtual-warehouse-isolation-and-shared-tenancy-economics]]) is really a fix for both at once.

**Isolation-first design versus the economics that erode it.** Snowflake's VW abstraction ([[virtual-warehouse-isolation-and-shared-tenancy-economics]]) was built to give each customer performance isolation — no shared workers, no partial retries, hard per-query boundaries. But the paper's own utilization numbers (up to 30% of VWs with CPU-usage variance as large as the mean) show that isolation has a real cost, and per-second billing turns that cost from an accounting footnote into something Snowflake itself has to absorb. The proposed fix — shared ephemeral storage across tenants — runs straight into the same consistent-hashing mechanic from [[ephemeral-storage-tiering-and-persistent-caching]]: a global hash space means one tenant's resize can degrade every other tenant's cache, so "share the resource" and "preserve the isolation" turn out to be in direct tension, not just a scheduling inconvenience.

**One immutable-file substrate, two unrelated payoffs.** [[snapshot-isolation-and-immutable-file-versioning]] and [[min-max-pruning-and-dynamic-data-skipping]] both exploit the same fact — table files in object storage are immutable, so changes always mean adding/removing whole files — but they extract completely different value from it. Versioning turns "can't overwrite a file" into MVCC, time travel, `UNDROP`, and cheap cloning. Pruning has nothing to do with versioning at all; it turns "each file/chunk has a known min/max" into the ability to skip reading files a query can't possibly need, whether decided statically ahead of a query or dynamically mid-join. The posts never connect these two explicitly, but both being downstream of "object storage files are immutable" is the reason Snowflake's storage design keeps paying for itself in more than one place.

**Schema declared vs. schema inferred, converging on the same columnar shape.** Native relational columns get their columnar/PAX-style layout and min-max metadata by explicit schema. [[schema-less-semi-structured-columnarization]] shows Snowflake reaching the *same* physical shape — separately-stored, compressed, min-max-tracked columns — for semi-structured `VARIANT` data, just by inferring common paths statistically instead of being told the schema. The Bloom-filter fix for paths that are common everywhere except a few files is a direct descendant of [[min-max-pruning-and-dynamic-data-skipping]] wearing a different hat: both exist purely to let the optimizer skip a file without opening it.

## Open questions
- Does the ephemeral storage system still strictly prioritize intermediate results over cached persistent data, or has that policy become configurable per use case? Vu asks this directly after finishing the 2020-paper deep dive and never resolves it against current Snowflake documentation.
- How does Snowflake actually handle the cost of pre-warmed-pool machines sitting idle before a customer's query arrives, now that VWs bill hourly for credit usage while running — the posts flag this as an open accounting question, not something the papers or later documentation settle.
- Does Snowflake currently support adjusting compute resources *during* a query's execution (intra-query elasticity), or is that still the "future direction" the 2020 paper described and never confirmed as shipped?
- Is the ephemeral storage system still disaggregated from compute today, in the sense the paper describes, or has that changed since 2020? Vu explicitly lists this as unresolved after his own follow-up research.
- **source gap** ([[virtual-warehouse-isolation-and-shared-tenancy-economics]]): the paper names the two hard problems in moving to shared ephemeral storage (joint tenant isolation on eviction; a global hash space causing cross-tenant rehash) but the posts never describe a shipped solution — only the "private address space" direction, left as future work.
- **source gap** ([[locality-aware-scheduling-and-work-stealing]]): the posts name the two scheduling extremes (place-with-cache vs. place-on-one-node) and say Snowflake wants a scheduler that balances them, but never spell out what that balancing algorithm actually does.
- **source gap** ([[cascades-optimizer-and-runtime-adaptivity]]): the posts say the optimizer is "Cascades-style, top-down, cost-based" but don't explain the search strategy (rule sets, memo structure, or how cost estimates are computed from the maintained statistics).
- **source gap** ([[schema-less-semi-structured-columnarization]]): the posts describe per-file statistical analysis deciding which paths are "common" enough to extract, but never spell out the actual threshold or algorithm used to make that determination.
- **source gap** ([[foundationdb]]): the posts name FoundationDB as the metadata store and say it tracks file additions/removals per table version, but don't describe how it's sharded, replicated, or scaled to Snowflake's metadata volume.

## Synthesis
Across a 2016 paper, a 2020 paper, and Vu's own 2024 re-synthesis, Snowflake's internals resolve into one repeated move: take a constraint imposed by object storage (immutable files, network latency, no server-local state) and turn it into a mechanism that pays for itself twice. Immutability becomes both [[snapshot-isolation-and-immutable-file-versioning|MVCC/time-travel/cloning]] and, separately, [[min-max-pruning-and-dynamic-data-skipping|pruning]]. Network latency to S3 becomes the reason for [[ephemeral-storage-tiering-and-persistent-caching|a custom local cache]], which in turn becomes the basis for [[locality-aware-scheduling-and-work-stealing|scheduling itself]]. And the desire to isolate customers cleanly via [[virtual-warehouse-isolation-and-shared-tenancy-economics|the VW abstraction]] runs headlong into the economics of per-second cloud billing, pushing Snowflake toward exactly the kind of shared-resource design the isolation model was built to avoid — a tension the papers name but, as of what Vu read, do not yet report as solved. Even reaching outside strict relational data, [[schema-less-semi-structured-columnarization|semi-structured columnarization]] and [[cascades-optimizer-and-runtime-adaptivity|runtime-adaptive optimization]] both turn out to be the same instinct applied further out: infer structure/strategy statistically rather than require it up front, and keep the option to correct course once real data is in hand.
