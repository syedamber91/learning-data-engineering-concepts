## Comparisons
**Five vendors, one pricing shape.** Fabric's CU, Synapse's DWU, Redshift's node-hour/RPU, BigQuery's slot, Databricks' DBU, and Snowflake's credit are five different names painted on the same abstraction: a vendor-defined compute unit sold at consumed-units × rate ([[cloud-warehouse-compute-pricing-abstractions]]). The differences that actually matter live in the rate, not the unit — region, cloud provider, edition/plan, workload type, and commitment level all move it independently, and Databricks alone splits the bill across two invoices (DBU plus a separate cloud-VM charge) unless you pick serverless. Storage pricing shows the same one-shape-many-costumes pattern one level down ([[cloud-warehouse-storage-pricing-dimensions]]): region, allocation granularity, data temperature, logical-vs-physical volume, and retention window all stack on top of each other, and only BigQuery combines temperature *and* volume-model into a genuinely four-way storage bill.

**Redshift's late-arriving decoupling vs. the other four's native separation.** BigQuery, Snowflake, and Databricks were all built shared-disk from day one or migrated to it early; Redshift started shared-nothing (the dc2 generation, storage bundled into the node) and only later bolted on Redshift Managed Storage for the RA3 generation ([[redshift-managed-storage-and-elastic-compute]]). That late split is exactly why Redshift's storage internals (a superblock indexing 1MB blocks, a two-level reference-count cache, log-based incremental commits) read as more elaborate machinery than Snowflake's or BigQuery's storage story elsewhere in this vault — Redshift had to retrofit onto an architecture that didn't assume disaggregation, whereas the others designed for it from the start.

**Vectorization's lone holdout, and what it buys instead.** BigQuery, Snowflake, and Databricks' Photon all execute vectorized; Redshift alone generates and compiles C++ per query ([[redshift-code-generation-and-self-tuning-operations]]). What Redshift gets in exchange for forgoing vectorization is a second, entirely separate axis of self-tuning most of the others don't publicly claim: ML-driven admission control and scheduling (AutoWLM), ML-driven key recommendation (Automatic Table Optimization), and an ML-forecast warm pool — vutr notes Redshift is, to his knowledge, the only OLAP system that documents using machine learning for its own background operations at all.

**Two philosophies for surviving millions of files: metadata as files vs. metadata as a database.** Iceberg/Delta/Hudi keep metadata *in* object storage as JSON/Avro files ([[olap-metadata-object-storage-vs-transactional-db]]), buying any-engine portability at the cost of ballooning metadata as file counts grow; Snowflake, BigQuery, and DuckLake keep metadata in a transactional store instead (FoundationDB, Spanner, or DuckLake's own approach), buying scale and real transactional guarantees at the cost of vendor lock-in and a single potential bottleneck. This is the same fork [[olap-metadata-object-storage-vs-transactional-db]] describes for tables in general, and it recurs almost verbatim inside materialized views: IVM theory (Timely Dataflow's completeness notification → Differential Dataflow's version reuse → DBSP's delta-stream formalization, [[incremental-view-maintenance-td-dd-dbsp]]) tells you *how* to compute only what changed, but every concrete cloud warehouse's actual internal IVM implementation is undocumented territory vutr flags as a real gap rather than papering over.

**Materialized views as the hinge between batch cost-control and real-time freshness.** [[materialized-view-tradeoffs-and-streaming-convergence]] reduces every MV decision to freshness-vs-cost — full refresh is simple but pays full compute every time, incremental refresh is cheaper per-refresh but not free — and at the freshness dial's maximum setting, the MV stops being distinguishable from a stream processor: Flink's Dynamic Tables, RisingWave, and ClickHouse's incremental MV are three real systems built around exactly that convergence. [[olap-real-time-freshness-mechanisms]] arrives at the same "how fresh, not how much" question from the storage-engine side instead of the query-semantics side: Pinot/Druid/StarRocks answer it with an in-memory buffer merged with cold disk data at query time, while BigQuery answers it with a hierarchical row-then-column flip — two different physical mechanisms converging on the same freshness goal that the MV/streaming convergence reaches from the query layer.

**Optimization technique stacks the same way everywhere, cost-control and query-execution alike.** [[olap-data-skipping-zone-maps-partitioning-clustering]] lays out a coarse-to-fine hierarchy — partitioning prunes whole subsets, clustering makes the zone maps inside a partition actually tight, zone maps skip individual blocks — that is the mechanical basis for the very same techniques [[olap-cost-control-client-side-practices]] recommends as spend-control levers, just viewed from the cost side rather than the mechanism side: the same clustering that tightens a zone map also slows ingestion and adds background compaction cost. [[olap-join-execution-and-broadcast-optimization]] rounds out the execution-side picture: OLAP abandons Nested Loop Join (an OLTP-shaped tool that needs a small side or an index neither side of a typical OLAP join has), leans on Hash Join and Sort-Merge Join executed in parallel across workers, and layers Broadcast Hash Join on top when one side is small enough — the same "avoid moving what you don't have to move" instinct that drives Redshift's Elastic Resize and the shared-disk architectures' whole reason for existing.

## Open questions
- How do BigQuery, Snowflake, or Redshift actually implement incremental materialized-view maintenance internally? vutr names the general theory (Timely Dataflow, Differential Dataflow, DBSP) but is explicit he could not find material connecting any specific cloud vendor's MV refresh mechanism to that theory. See [[incremental-view-maintenance-td-dd-dbsp]].
- Does DuckLake's transactional-metadata design actually solve the "central store becomes a bottleneck" problem the way it claims to, or does it just relocate the same scaling ceiling that any single metadata database eventually hits? vutr flags this as something he had not yet examined. See [[olap-metadata-object-storage-vs-transactional-db]].
- Has cheap, elastic cloud compute caused teams to under-invest in data modeling compared to the on-premises era, when disk cost forced careful up-front design? vutr raises this as his own open reflection, not a documented finding, at the end of his cost-savings piece. See [[olap-cost-control-client-side-practices]].
- **source gap**: the posts describe BigQuery's incremental MV refresh as limited to specific join shapes (e.g., a JOIN where only the right side gained rows) but don't spell out the general rule for which query shapes qualify for incremental refresh across engines versus which always fall back to full recompute.
- **source gap**: the posts name FoundationDB (Snowflake) and Google Spanner (BigQuery) as the transactional metadata stores behind those two engines but don't spell out how either store is actually queried or kept consistent under concurrent writes.
- **source gap**: the posts describe Redshift's AutoWLM converting a query plan into a "vector format" fed to an ML model, and separately describe the Query Predictor Framework training that model with XGBoost, but don't spell out what features make up that vector or how the model's predicted execution time and memory need translate into the actual admission decision.

## Synthesis
Read together, these seven posts are less about any single engine's internals and more about the fact that "cloud data warehouse" names one recurring architectural bet — separate storage from compute, bill for consumed units, optimize by skipping data you don't need to touch — instantiated five slightly different ways, with Redshift as the interesting outlier that arrived at the same bet late and from a different starting architecture. The pricing layer ([[cloud-warehouse-compute-pricing-abstractions]], [[cloud-warehouse-storage-pricing-dimensions]]) turns out to be exactly as multi-dimensional as the engines' internals are, and vutr's own conclusion — that understanding a pricing model is one of the most time-consuming parts of working with these systems — is itself evidence that the abstraction vendors sell ("just write SQL, we'll handle the rest") is incomplete without also understanding what you're being charged for and why ([[olap-cost-control-client-side-practices]]). Underneath the pricing, the same handful of mechanical ideas keep resurfacing in different costumes: skip data you don't need (zone maps, partitioning, clustering — [[olap-data-skipping-zone-maps-partitioning-clustering]]), move less data than you'd naively have to (broadcast joins, Redshift's Elastic Resize, RMS's incremental commits), and answer "how fresh" as its own axis separate from "how much" (real-time buffers, hierarchical storage, materialized views converging into stream processors — [[olap-real-time-freshness-mechanisms]], [[materialized-view-tradeoffs-and-streaming-convergence]]). Redshift's storage and compute internals ([[redshift-managed-storage-and-elastic-compute]], [[redshift-code-generation-and-self-tuning-operations]]) are the deepest single-vendor thread in this batch precisely because vutr spent a dedicated 8-hour deep dive on it, and what falls out is a system that chose code generation over vectorization and ML-driven self-tuning over static configuration — a genuinely different bet than BigQuery, Snowflake, or Databricks made, even though all four now sell the same "storage and compute, separately billed, separately scaled" pitch on the marketing page.
