## Comparisons
**The three CDC types sit on one impact-vs-complexity spectrum, and it's the same spectrum that runs through "how do I know what's new."** [[query-based-cdc]] is cheapest to build — a scheduled query against an `updated_timestamp` — but structurally blind to DELETEs and dependent on a column that might not exist. [[trigger-based-cdc]] fixes the blindness by writing every mutation into a shadow table via database triggers, at the cost of a guaranteed double write on the source for every transaction. [[log-based-cdc]] reads the transaction log directly — the WAL, redo log, or binlog that write-ahead logging already guarantees is complete and durable — buying the lowest source impact and full completeness at the price of the highest operational complexity: logical replication configuration, elevated permissions, and real streaming infrastructure (a connector like [[debezium]], a broker, downstream consumers). Zoom out and this is just three specific answers to a bigger question Vu asks separately in his sourcing framework: [[incremental-extraction-strategies]] lists timestamp filters, overlapping date windows, Kafka-style offsets, CDC, and full refresh as five ways to answer "what's new," and CDC only earns its complexity when freshness requirements and a missing timestamp column both push you there — it is one tool in that kit, not the only one.

**Two different mechanisms both claim to move data reliably, for different reasons.** [[log-based-cdc]] is built to synchronize a downstream copy of a source database with minimal disruption to that source. The [[outbox-pattern-and-dual-write-problem]] solves a narrower, structural problem — a service doing two writes (save state, publish event) that can fail independently — by making both writes atomic in the service's own database and using CDC (trigger- or log-based) purely as the message relay that drains the outbox table into a broker. One is about extracting a source you don't own; the other is about a service extracting its *own* state changes reliably. They converge on the same machinery only because both need "watch a table, ship what changes" as a primitive.

**Performance cost and operational cost are not the same axis, and [[cdc-operational-considerations]] keeps them separate on purpose.** Performance-wise, log-based is gentlest on the source, trigger-based taxes every write, and query-based taxes every read — this ranking matches [[source-performance-impact-by-type]]'s broader claim that a database source's biggest risk is query pressure, best solved with a read replica (CDC via logical replication is explicitly called gentler than periodic bulk exports here). Operationally, though, log-based CDC is the *most* demanding: it needs lag monitoring, alerting, and failure-recovery playbooks that trigger- and query-based CDC mostly don't, precisely because it's a real streaming system with more moving parts. Security follows the same logic across all three — the CDC tool's credentials should follow least privilege, which is the same principle [[source-access-trust-boundary]] applies to every source, CDC or not.

**Schema-change severity and data-quality contracts are two different failure surfaces the source can hand you, and neither is solved by CDC alone.** [[schema-change-severity-and-detection]]'s taxonomy — additive (safe) through rename/drop (breaks queries) through type change (breaks casts) through semantic change (breaks nothing structurally, just meaning) — applies whether or not the pipeline is CDC-based; a schema registry helps for streaming sources specifically, which is exactly where log-based CDC's Kafka transport lives. [[data-quality-contract-with-source]] is a level below that: even a structurally unchanged schema can carry bad values, and no CDC type, however complete, can catch a null rate that silently crept up or a batch of late-arriving records. The two problems need two different defenses — schema validation at ingestion for one, distribution/count monitoring for the other — layered on top of whichever extraction mechanism is doing the pulling.

## Open questions
- When exactly does log-based CDC's complexity stop being worth it versus simpler extraction? Vu names the trade-off (lowest impact, highest complexity) but doesn't give a concrete threshold — table size, freshness requirement, or team maturity — for when to reach for it over timestamp-based extraction.
- **source gap** ([[log-based-cdc]]): the source names the log reader and log publisher as CDC-connector responsibilities (citing Debezium) but doesn't spell out how a connector like Debezium actually parses a proprietary log format or handles connector failure/restart mid-stream.
- **source gap** ([[outbox-pattern-and-dual-write-problem]]): the source describes CDC monitoring the outbox table for new INSERTs but doesn't spell out how the relay avoids re-publishing an event it's already delivered, or how the outbox table itself is pruned once events are relayed.
- **source gap** ([[schema-change-severity-and-detection]]): the source names schema-registry compatibility rules as rejecting "incompatible" changes but doesn't spell out what the compatibility modes actually are or how a specific proposed schema is checked against a prior version.
- **source gap** ([[exactly-once-and-missing-data-detection]]): the source says deduplication's hard part is "finding the right key" but doesn't give a method for discovering that key when it isn't explicit in the source, beyond "profile the data or ask around."
- How do teams operationalize turning an informal understanding with a source team into an actual enforced [[data-quality-contract-with-source|data quality contract]], rather than a one-time conversation that quietly goes stale?
- [[source-delete-handling]] names SCD Type 2-style snapshot comparison as an alternative to CDC for catching hard deletes, but the source doesn't say how a team decides between paying for CDC versus running periodic full-snapshot reconciliation for delete detection alone.

## Synthesis
The organizing idea across both posts is that the source is the one part of the pipeline you don't fully control, and every mechanism here exists to answer a version of "what changed, and how do I find out without breaking the thing I don't own." [[query-based-cdc]], [[trigger-based-cdc]], and [[log-based-cdc]] are three points on a single spectrum trading source impact against build complexity, with [[log-based-cdc]] reading the same write-ahead log that guarantees database crash recovery — repurposed as the most complete, least invasive CDC source once you're willing to run real streaming infrastructure (often via a connector like [[debezium]]) to consume it. That log-reading primitive turns out to be reusable: the [[outbox-pattern-and-dual-write-problem]] uses it to solve a distributed-systems problem (the dual write) rather than a data-integration one, and CDC's completeness doubles as [[cdc-audit-log-and-compliance|regulatory audit trail]] almost for free. But CDC is only one answer inside a much larger sourcing discipline. [[pull-vs-push-source-types]] and [[source-access-trust-boundary]] determine what infrastructure a source demands before extraction logic even starts; [[incremental-extraction-strategies]] places CDC alongside timestamp filters, overlap windows, offsets, and full refresh as competing ways to answer "what's new," governed by Vu's "don't over-engineer the freshness" principle; [[source-performance-impact-by-type]] and [[source-retention-and-replayability]] shape how gently and how far back you can extract; and [[schema-change-severity-and-detection]], [[exactly-once-and-missing-data-detection]], [[source-delete-handling]], and [[data-quality-contract-with-source]] are the four distinct ways a source can quietly hand you wrong data even when every task in the pipeline reports success. [[cdc-operational-considerations]] and [[source-availability-and-sla]] close the loop: whatever extraction method you chose, someone still has to monitor it, alert on it, and know how long the business can tolerate it being down. The throughline is that none of this is purely technical — nearly every mechanism here (read replicas, schema registries, least-privilege credentials, soft-delete negotiation, data contracts) is really a negotiated agreement with a team that owns something you depend on but don't control.
