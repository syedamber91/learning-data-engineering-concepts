## Comparisons
**Classic replication vs [[tiered-storage-kip-405]] vs diskless.** These are three points on one axis: how much state a broker carries. [[leader-follower-replication]] buys durability and failover on local disks, at the price of network-heavy rebalancing and multi-AZ deployments paying at least (2/3 + 2)x the cross-AZ unit price — Vu's worked example puts three r6i.large brokers at ~$272/month in VMs against ~$4,050/month in cross-AZ traffic. Tiered storage offloads historical segments to HDFS/S3/GCS but the local hot tier, the replication between brokers, and the data movement on membership change all persist — KIP-405 loosens the compute-storage coupling, it does not remove it. The diskless generation ([[diskless-kafka-trade-off-framework]]) goes the rest of the way: object storage's eleven-nines durability replaces broker replication entirely, brokers become stateless compute, and [[partition-reassignment-and-cluster-balancing|reassignment]] collapses into a metadata edit. The price is latency — writing to object storage is slower than appending to a local segment — and that is the trade every vendor then has to take a position on.

**WarpStream vs AutoMQ vs Bufstream.** All three land on object storage but disagree on almost everything else. [[warpstream-stateless-agent-architecture]] and Bufstream are ground-up protocol reimplementations, leaderless (any agent takes any write), and ack only after the ~250ms/8MiB buffer lands in object storage — WarpStream's produce p99 is ~400ms, end-to-end ~1s; the founders' pitch is explicit that if you tolerate that, streaming cost drops 5-10x per GiB. The leaderless choice demands an external transactional metadata store (DynamoDB/Spanner/etcd/Postgres) to re-sequence batches, and WarpStream's consistent-hashing cache is, per the sources' own admission, a fall back to leader-based ideas to recover data locality. [[automq-wal-shared-storage]] refuses the latency trade: it reuses Kafka's code (100% compatibility), keeps partition leaders and KRaft metadata, and acks once the message persists in a small EBS WAL, flushing to S3 asynchronously — then offers its own internal dial between EBS WAL (latency-sensitive) and S3 WAL (cross-AZ cost near zero, worse latency, more VMs). Bufstream differentiates on what the broker *does* rather than where bytes live: [[broker-side-schema-and-semantic-validation]] and writing topics directly as Iceberg tables ([[kafka-iceberg-zero-etl]]), fully inside the customer's VPC where WarpStream's BYOC still routes metadata to its cloud.

**Push vs pull consumption.** LinkedIn chose pull against the push-based norm of Scribe and Flume because a consumer should retrieve messages at the maximum rate it can afford rather than be flooded — falling behind means catching up at your own pace, and pulls batch naturally ([[pull-based-consumption-and-offset-commit]], [[message-batching-and-compression]]). The unusual corollary is that the consumer doesn't track its own position: the broker records committed offsets in `__consumer_offsets`, which is exactly what makes ownership transfer during [[consumer-group-rebalancing]] resumable. But pull is not a law of nature — [[northguard-segment-level-replication]], from the same company fifteen years later, pushes data to consumers within a client-declared budget. The notes present that reversal without ceremony: the pull decision was a bet on 2010-era consumer heterogeneity, and LinkedIn itself was willing to re-litigate it once it abandoned the Kafka protocol anyway.

**Client-side vs broker-side data governance.** Classic Kafka's broker sees messages as an array of bytes — a deliberate LinkedIn-era performance decision sitting alongside batching and [[page-cache-sequential-io-and-zero-copy|zero-copy]] — so validation lives in clients mediated by a schema registry. The notes' verdict is blunt: if data governance is opt-in, it's NOT data governance — compatibility checks can be switched off, auto-registration is on by default, and a Protobuf rename that keeps the tag number passes the registry check while breaking every JSON-keyed consumer downstream. Bufstream inverts this: the broker validates structure and even semantics (Protovalidate CEL rules like `age` between 1 and 200), burning broker CPU and a little latency in exchange for clients that can never forget. [[broker-side-schema-and-semantic-validation]] frames it as flexibility traded for reliability; the same schema-as-gatekeeper stance is what lets AutoMQ's Table Topic evolve Iceberg schemas without interrupting writes ([[kafka-iceberg-zero-etl]]).

**Three answers to the rebalancing problem.** Because replicas live on local disks, adding a broker balances nothing — it sits idle until data physically moves ([[partition-reassignment-and-cluster-balancing]]). The notes stake out three escapes. Cruise Control automates the *planning* (workload model + goal optimizer) but not the physics: it acts on a snapshot, so under fluctuating traffic its decisions go stale mid-execution. AutoMQ deletes the data movement: with brokers stateless over object storage, reassignment is a KRaft metadata edit ([[zookeeper-to-kraft-metadata-management]]), and AutoBalancer can self-balance continuously precisely because moves are cheap. [[northguard-segment-level-replication]] shrinks the unit instead: replicate 1GB segments rather than whole partitions, so a new broker just receives the *next active segment* and ingest traffic follows with zero movement. Automate the plan, delete the movement, or shrink the moved unit — the notes treat these as genuinely different philosophies, not variants of one fix.

## Open questions
- Will the Diskless Topics KIP (KIP-1150) actually land in Apache Kafka — and if upstream Kafka goes diskless natively, what remains of the WarpStream/AutoMQ/Bufstream value propositions beyond the features ([[broker-side-schema-and-semantic-validation]], [[kafka-iceberg-zero-etl]]) layered on top? The notes flag the KIP as "still under discussion" and go no further.
- Is leaderless architecture viable at scale, or does it always creep back toward leaders? [[warpstream-stateless-agent-architecture]]'s consistent-hashing cache is described as an effective fall back to leader-based ideas, and [[automq-wal-shared-storage]] kept leaders outright — but the notes never settle whether leaderless has an enduring advantage once its coordinator and data-locality costs are paid.
- Where exactly is the latency floor for object-storage Kafka? The notes give the dial (shorter buffers = more PUTs = higher cost, or pay for S3 Express One) and the decision rule (EBS WAL for anti-fraud/finance, S3 WAL for logs), but not whether latency-sensitive workloads can *ever* move fully diskless.
- Can Bufstream's single-copy design — the Iceberg table *is* the topic — serve both row-by-row streaming consumers and analytical query engines from one physical layout without one workload degrading the other? [[kafka-iceberg-zero-etl]] presents the 2-for-1 economics but not the contention story.
- Does Northguard's bet generalize? The notes predict it likely won't replace Kafka outside LinkedIn — few companies share its requirements or resources — yet [[northguard-segment-level-replication]] also shows segment-level replication solving problems ([[partition-reassignment-and-cluster-balancing]]) that every large operator has. Whether protocol compatibility is truly non-negotiable for everyone else is left open, as is how far [[zero-downtime-migration-dual-write]]-style tooling lowers the switching cost.

## Synthesis
Kafka began as LinkedIn's bet that a dumb, fast, append-only log could carry a company's entire event traffic: messages with no IDs, addressed by offset in 1GB segment files ([[log-segments-and-offset-addressing]]), written and read sequentially so the OS page cache and `sendfile()` do the heavy lifting ([[page-cache-sequential-io-and-zero-copy]]), with [[message-batching-and-compression|batching]] amortizing both the network hop and the disk write, keys deciding placement ([[message-key-partitioning-strategies]]), the producer choosing its durability contract via acks ([[producer-send-path-and-acks]]), and consumers *pulling* at their own pace with the broker tracking committed offsets ([[pull-based-consumption-and-offset-commit]]). Scale-out came from the same primitives — partitions as the unit of parallelism divided among [[consumer-groups-and-partition-assignment|consumer groups]], with [[consumer-group-rebalancing]] handling membership churn, [[leader-follower-replication]] buying durability on local disks, and metadata moving from ZooKeeper into the KRaft quorum ([[zookeeper-to-kraft-metadata-management]]). Operations at the extreme proved the design both magnificent and expensive: LinkedIn ran 7 trillion messages a day on multi-tier clusters with count-based auditing ([[linkedin-multi-tier-clusters-and-audit]]), while PayPal and everyone else discovered that [[partition-reassignment-and-cluster-balancing|rebalancing]] means moving real data, sometimes for days. The cloud turned those costs into line items — cross-AZ replication fees dwarfing VM costs — and the responses form a clean progression: [[tiered-storage-kip-405]] offloads history but keeps brokers stateful; the diskless wave ([[diskless-kafka-trade-off-framework]]) puts everything in object storage and splits into leaderless protocol rewrites ([[warpstream-stateless-agent-architecture]]) versus leader-keeping code reuse ([[automq-wal-shared-storage]]); Bufstream pushes the broker up the value chain with [[broker-side-schema-and-semantic-validation|schema and semantic validation]]; shared data ([[kafka-iceberg-zero-etl]]) makes the topic *be* the Iceberg table; LinkedIn abandons the protocol itself for segment-level replication ([[northguard-segment-level-replication]]); and [[zero-downtime-migration-dual-write|dual-write migration]] is the bridge that makes any of these moves survivable. The through-line: the offset-addressed log abstraction has never changed — what keeps changing is where the bytes live and who pays for moving them.
