## Comparisons
**Text vs binary, row vs column: Parquet is the end of a progression, not a standalone design.** [[text-vs-binary-file-formats]] lays out the chain directly: CSV and JSON are human-legible but row-oriented and (for JSON) verbose and slow to parse; [[apache-avro]] fixes the schema-evolution and compactness problems with a binary, writer's-schema/reader's-schema split, but stays row-oriented; Parquet is the answer to the one weakness Avro doesn't touch — analytical scans that only need a handful of columns out of many. Its [[parquet-origin|origin]] as a Twitter/Cloudera project committed it, from v1.0 onward, to the PAX hybrid layout (horizontal row groups, columnar within) rather than either extreme — see the storage-models topic's [[pax-hybrid-layout]] concept for the general PAX framing this topic's [[row-group]] and [[column-chunk]] entities instantiate concretely.

**Encoding choice is data-shape dependent, and the writer is more opinionated than people expect.** [[rle-dictionary]] is the aggressive default Parquet applies to *every* column type except BOOLEAN — not a neutral option among equals. It only works well below a cardinality/dictionary-size threshold, past which the writer falls back (Java Parquet specifically falls back to `DELTA_BINARY_PACKED` for INT32/INT64, not the spec's nominal PLAIN fallback). [[delta-encodings]] beat dictionary encoding outright on sorted timestamps or auto-incrementing keys (`DELTA_BINARY_PACKED`), on short-string columns where PLAIN's fixed length-prefix wastes space (`DELTA_LENGTH_BYTE_ARRAY`), and on prefix-sharing strings like URLs (`DELTA_BYTE_ARRAY`). [[byte-stream-split-encoding]] is a different kind of lever entirely — it doesn't shrink data itself, it reorganizes FLOAT/DOUBLE bytes so a *later* compressor can actually find patterns in the exponent/sign bytes it would otherwise miss, mixed in with near-random mantissa bytes. None of this is automatic: the luminousmen/Vu piece's practical advice is that RLE and dictionary encoding only pay off if the data is sorted or clustered first — the encoding scheme is necessary but not sufficient.

**Row-group sizing is the same lever pulling three different formats in three different directions.** Parquet's own guidance (128MB-1GB, or a narrower 128MB-512MB in the optimization-focused piece) balances I/O efficiency against parallelism and metadata overhead — and collides badly with large column values like vector embeddings, where a 4KiB value packs roughly 500x fewer rows into the same row-group budget as an 8-byte scalar, forcing more row groups, more metadata, and thinner per-row-group compression samples ([[row-group]]). The three successor formats treat this as the central design fork: [[lance-file-format|Lance]] deletes row groups entirely (each column paced independently); [[nimble-file-format|Nimble]] keeps them (so the tension persists, just with a better-placed footer); [[vortex-file-format|Vortex]] refuses to choose, and can express either shape — or its own default adaptive per-column chunking — through composable layouts.

**Metadata bloat and random access are really the same underlying problem approached from two angles.** [[footer-filemetadata|FileMetadata]] encoded in Thrift/Protobuf can't be randomly accessed, so a query needing one column's schema out of a thousand-column feature table still deserializes the whole footer — the "wide table" problem. [[page|Pages]] being the unit of decompression means a single embedding lookup still pays to decompress a whole page and decode past its neighbors — the "random access" problem ([[parquet-random-access-limitation]]). Lance, Nimble, and Vortex all fix the metadata half the same way in spirit (per-column protobuf messages for Lance, FlatBuffers for Nimble and Vortex — all designed for access without full deserialization), but diverge sharply on the random-access half: Lance splits by value size (tiny miniblocks vs. transparently-compressed full-zip chunks for large values), Nimble doesn't try (explicitly optimized for sequential decode, not point lookups), and Vortex leans on "transparent" encodings plus compute kernels that operate on compressed data directly, claiming the largest (100x) random-access win of the three.

**Three different bets on how an extensible format avoids fragmenting.** Lance is an open spec inviting multiple implementations — the same governance choice Parquet itself made, and the same fragmentation risk that comes with it, since keeping Java/Python/C++/Go/Rust readers and writers in sync is genuinely hard. Nimble goes the opposite way: one canonical C++ library, with Meta explicitly discouraging reimplementation — consistency by fiat rather than by design-for-divergence. Vortex splits the difference with a plan (not yet implemented) to embed WebAssembly decompression kernels *inside* the file itself, so a reader without native support for a new encoding could still decode it via the file's own embedded logic — an attempt to get encoding extensibility without paying either Lance's fragmentation risk or Nimble's single-implementation bottleneck.

## Open questions
- Will Parquet actually evolve fast enough to keep the "won't be replaced soon" verdict true? The sources point to two concrete efforts — a FlatBuffer-based footer proposal and ongoing work on more state-of-the-art encodings — but never confirm either has landed, and the posts' own tone toward Parquet's survival is a hedge ("I believe...") rather than a settled claim.
- Is F3, cited across the alternative-formats piece as aiming to "eliminate the need for custom file formats" via a general-purpose encoding API, anything more than a research prototype? Vu says outright he's "betting on" its production-ready implementation, which means the posts themselves treat this as unresolved.
- Will Lance's open-spec governance actually fragment across implementations the way Parquet's has, or is it too young for that risk to have shown up yet?
- Does Vortex's embedded-WebAssembly-decompression-kernel plan ever ship — and does it work in practice, or only in the qualified sense the source itself uses ("if it works")?
- **source gap**: the posts assert that Thrift and Protocol Buffers "do not support random access" into serialized metadata, but never explain the wire-format reason why (e.g., sequential field-by-field parsing) — only that FlatBuffers avoids the problem.
- **source gap**: [[rle-dictionary]]'s fallback threshold is given only as an illustrative "e.g., 1MB" — the posts don't state what real-world Parquet writers (parquet-mr, arrow-rs) actually default to in production.
- **source gap**: [[byte-stream-split-encoding]] is explained mechanically (why it should help compression) but its actual compression-ratio payoff is never quantified in any of the posts.
- Two of the eight raw posts in this batch contributed no citable technical content: `groupby-8-headline.md` only links out to a third-party Parquet explainer without summarizing it, and `parquet-fundamentals-in-3-mins.md` is a video stub with no transcript text. This synthesis is grounded entirely in the other six.

## Synthesis
Parquet exists to solve one problem text-based and row-oriented binary formats structurally can't: analytical queries that only touch a handful of a table's columns ([[text-vs-binary-file-formats]]). Its answer, since its 2013 [[parquet-origin|origin]] as a Twitter/Cloudera project, has always been the PAX hybrid layout — horizontal [[row-group]]s, columnar [[column-chunk]]s within them, [[page]]s as the actual unit of encoding and compression — with [[footer-filemetadata|FileMetadata]] in the footer carrying the min/max statistics that make [[predicate-pushdown]] possible. On top of that physical shape sits a genuinely rich encoding toolkit driven by [[parquet-physical-and-logical-types|the physical/logical type split]] — aggressive [[rle-dictionary|dictionary encoding]] by default, [[rle-bit-packing-hybrid|RLE/bit-packing]] underneath it and underneath [[definition-repetition-levels|Dremel-style nested-data shredding]], [[delta-encodings|delta schemes]] for sorted or prefix-sharing data, and [[byte-stream-split-encoding]] for floating point — none of which pays off without the write-time discipline [[layout-over-code]] insists on (right row-group size, sorted data, chosen compression codec).

But the design that made Parquet win in an I/O-bound, HDFS-shaped world is now visibly strained by two things happening at once: the [[cpu-bound-lakehouse|CPU, not I/O, being the new bottleneck]], and AI workloads exposing [[parquet-random-access-limitation|Parquet's weak random access]] and the wide-table metadata bloat that comes from Thrift/Protobuf's lack of random access into the footer itself. [[lance-file-format|Lance]], [[nimble-file-format|Nimble]], and [[vortex-file-format|Vortex]] are three separate answers built by three separate organizations (LanceDB, Meta, SpiralDB) that agree on almost nothing except the diagnosis — they disagree on whether to keep row groups, how to solve random access, how to structure metadata, and how to keep their own encodings from fragmenting the way Parquet's implementations have. The consistent verdict across the sources, though, is that none of this replaces Parquet soon: its decade of near-universal engine support is a moat none of the newer formats can match yet, so the more likely near-term outcome is a split — Parquet for general analytics, a newer format for AI-shaped workloads, bridged by whatever interoperability layer a team is willing to build.
