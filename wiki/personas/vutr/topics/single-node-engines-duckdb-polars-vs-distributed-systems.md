## Comparisons
**One machine, then not, then maybe again.** The arc runs cleanly across both raw posts: a single machine was originally enough because data volume was small and mostly OLTP-shaped; the internet turned data machine-generated in volume and structure, which is the problem Google built [[mapreduce-origins-and-limits|MapReduce]] to solve — persisting every intermediate result to disk so a company could reliably process planet-scale data across unreliable machines. That reliability trade cost throughput, especially for iterative ML workloads, so [[spark-in-memory-model-and-overhead|Spark]] answered with in-memory processing and lineage-based fault tolerance instead of disk replication. [[cloud-data-warehouse-elt-shift|Cloud data warehouses]] rose alongside Spark by abstracting infrastructure entirely and pushing transformation logic into SQL (ETL→ELT). Then, per the second post's central claim, single-node engines came back around: not by rejecting any of this history, but by filling a market gap that opened between Pandas/NumPy's GIL ceiling and the overkill of cluster-based processing for anything short of genuinely large data ([[single-node-engine-market-gap]]).

**DuckDB's internals vs. the market-gap argument.** These are genuinely two different pieces of grounding from two different posts, and it's worth being precise about which post supports which claim. The DuckDB-specific post gives real internals: an embedded, client-server-free architecture modeled on SQLite ([[duckdb-embedded-analytics-model]]); a vectorized, push-based execution engine descended from the 2005 MonetDB/X100 paper ([[vectorized-execution-engine]], [[push-based-vs-pull-based-dataflow]]); a custom in-memory `Vector` format with four physical representations ([[duckdb-vector-formats]]); row-group-bound Parquet parallelism ([[parquet-row-group-parallelism]]); and ACID via custom MVCC ([[duckdb-acid-and-mvcc]]). The later "why single-node engines" post explicitly disclaims doing any of that for either DuckDB or Polars — it states outright that it won't dive deep into their internals, since its research is on the *motivation* behind these systems. That's why Polars, in this wiki, has real coverage of its market position but almost no coverage of its own execution mechanics — the raw material simply doesn't go there (see [[polars]]).

**Vectorization shows up twice, for different reasons.** The DuckDB post frames vectorization as an internals choice inherited from a specific 2005 paper and shared with BigQuery, Snowflake, and Databricks/Photon. The single-node-engine post frames vectorization as one leg of a *hardware* argument — AVX-512's SIMD instructions let a CPU add 16 pairs of numbers at once, and software that exploits that (vectorized execution) is one of three concrete reasons DuckDB and Polars are fast on a laptop today. Same mechanism, cited independently by two posts for two different purposes — internals lineage in one, hardware-trend payoff in the other.

**Three answers to "why do single-node engines work now," each attacking a different bottleneck.** The market gap ([[single-node-engine-market-gap]]) explains *when* a single-node engine is the right choice (medium data, where Pandas is too weak and Spark is overkill). Hardware trends ([[single-node-processing]]) explain *why it's technically possible now* — 128GB laptop RAM, 100x faster NVMe over HDD, AVX-512 SIMD. DevEx ([[devex-as-adoption-driver]]) explains *why it actually gets adopted* — a `pip install` and a sub-30-second first query beat a technically-superior tool with a slower on-ramp, and Arrow's zero-copy reach into remote storage ([[arrow-enables-location-independent-queries]]) removes the "but my data isn't local" objection. None of the three is sufficient alone in the source's own account — the "why single-node engines" post presents them as three necessary legs of one argument, not competing explanations.

## Open questions
- **source gap** ([[push-based-vs-pull-based-dataflow]]): the source states pull-based execution caused "code duplication" and blocked operators from running separately from the tree plan, per DuckDB's own team, but doesn't explain the underlying mechanism of *why* a pull interface forces that duplication.
- **source gap** ([[duckdb-vector-formats]]): Dictionary Vectors are described as "emitted by the storage when decompressing from dictionary," but the source doesn't spell out when or how DuckDB decides to dictionary-encode data in the first place.
- The DuckDB-specific post is explicitly "20% how-to, 80% introduction" and says a deeper technical dive into vectorization or push/pull dataflow may come later — this wiki only has what that introductory post covers, not the deeper mechanics the author flagged as a possible future article.
- The single-node-engine post is explicitly framed as the author's own online research on *motivation*, not a technical profile of DuckDB or Polars internals — it says so directly ("this article won't dive deep into DuckDB or Polars"). That means Polars in particular has almost no grounded internals coverage in this wiki (see [[polars]]), and any future post specifically about Polars mechanics would need its own raw source.
- Neither post gives a concrete dataset-size threshold for "medium" — the market-gap argument ([[single-node-engine-market-gap]]) is qualitative throughout: Pandas is "too limited," Spark is "overkill," but no GB/row-count boundary is given for where one stops and the other starts.
- The source names Apache Arrow's zero-copy integration and remote-storage reach as one leg of the DevEx argument, but explicitly defers Arrow's own mechanics to the author's separate Arrow article, which isn't one of the raw posts in this group — see the existing [[apache-arrow]] topic for that grounding instead.
- The single-node-engine post's own Note 1 flags a real limitation worth surfacing: it says the historical account (Google's motivations, MapReduce's rise and fall, Spark's adoption curve) "is based entirely on my research online, so it might not reflect the context at that time" — an explicit author caveat about the History section's reliability, not something this wiki should treat as first-hand engineering experience the way the DuckDB internals post reads.

## Synthesis
Read together, the two dense posts in this group tell a two-layer story. The historical layer (from "why single-node engines") is a chain of bottleneck-and-fix: one machine was enough until the internet made data machine-generated in volume and structure; [[mapreduce-origins-and-limits|MapReduce]] solved distribution and fault tolerance at the cost of disk-bound iteration; [[spark-in-memory-model-and-overhead|Spark]] solved iteration at the cost of deep operational complexity; [[cloud-data-warehouse-elt-shift|cloud warehouses]] solved operational complexity for a price and a pricing model you still have to understand; and single-node engines close a gap none of the above ever covered — the medium-sized dataset that's too big for Pandas' GIL ceiling and needlessly expensive for a cluster ([[single-node-engine-market-gap]]). That gap closes not on the strength of any one factor but three together: hardware finally makes it possible on one machine ([[single-node-processing]]), and Developer Experience — an install, not a deployment — is what actually drives people to try it ([[devex-as-adoption-driver]]), with Arrow quietly removing the objection that a single machine can't reach remote data ([[arrow-enables-location-independent-queries]]). The internals layer (from the DuckDB-specific post) is where the "how" behind one of those two named engines actually lives: embedded, server-less operation ([[duckdb-embedded-analytics-model]]), a vectorized push-based engine descended from a real 2005 systems paper ([[vectorized-execution-engine]], [[push-based-vs-pull-based-dataflow]]), a purpose-built in-memory vector format ([[duckdb-vector-formats]]), row-group-bound Parquet parallelism ([[parquet-row-group-parallelism]]), and ACID guarantees via custom MVCC ([[duckdb-acid-and-mvcc]]). Polars sits in the market-gap story as DuckDB's peer, but this wiki has almost none of its internals — the source itself says that's out of scope here, which is a real, named gap rather than an oversight.
