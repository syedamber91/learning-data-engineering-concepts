## Comparisons
**"Sink-first" turns out to have a lot more inside it than the framework's original one-paragraph-per-question shape suggested.** [[sink-first-requirements-gathering]] is the entry point — business purpose, data model, output shape, staleness, usage pattern, retention, atomicity — but Vu's dedicated serving-layer piece shows each of those questions has its own depth once you stop treating "the sink" as one stop: [[data-grain-and-serving-storage-shape]] works out that "raw" means a different grain to a data scientist, a BI analyst, and an application backend, and that grain can only be aggregated up, never decomposed down; [[physical-layout-partitioning-clustering-and-compaction]] shows the write-side cost of the very partitioning/clustering choices the framework recommends, plus a small-file/compaction problem the framework never mentions; [[concurrency-and-resource-isolation-in-serving]], [[safe-writes-and-schema-evolution-in-serving]], and [[data-access-control-layers]] are entirely new axes — concurrency, write-idempotency, schema evolution, and access tiering — that simply didn't exist in the original snapshot's one-line "sink" summary. [[source-constraints-and-schema-risk]] stays comparatively flat by contrast: it is still "the part you don't control," and the deeper posts add specificity (read replicas, rate limits, schema-change notification) without restructuring the question the way the sink side got restructured.

**Batch-vs-stream is reframed from a binary choice into two different constraint problems.** [[batch-vs-stream-throughput-and-latency]] shows batch reduces to a single number — throughput, data volume over time window — while streaming splits into two: latency (per-record) and throughput (sustained rate under spiky load), which interact and trade against each other via batching. This lines up with, and gives mechanism to, [[clarifying-questions-before-tools]]'s satirical inverse: reaching for Spark and streaming by default, regardless of whether the actual throughput/latency numbers justify it, is exactly the anti-pattern the "how to build a pipeline that sucks" piece mocks.

**Idempotency shows up in three different guises across the framework, at three different levels of who has to build it.** At the processing/orchestration level, [[idempotency]] is something the pipeline author has to design in deliberately — overwrite instead of append, MERGE/upsert, avoid non-deterministic functions, end-to-end or it doesn't count. At the serving layer, [[safe-writes-and-schema-evolution-in-serving]] shows the same problem restated as "what does the *sink itself* do with a duplicate logical write" — four absorption strategies (MERGE/upsert, overwrite-by-partition, dedup-on-write, append-plus-dedup-on-read), the last of which pushes the cost onto every consumer instead of solving it once. And at the mechanism level, [[snowflake-copy-into-and-event-driven-orchestration]] shows a case where none of that hand-built logic is even necessary — Snowflake's `COPY INTO` tracks which files it has already loaded, so idempotency arrives for free as a property of the load mechanism itself. Three different answers to "what happens if this runs twice," and the framework's real lesson is that which one applies depends entirely on which system you're standing in front of.

**Failure recovery, correctness testing, and data quality checking are three different lines of defense that colloquially get flattened into "make sure the pipeline works."** [[pipeline-failure-recovery-and-checkpointing]] is about crash recovery — does the pipeline resume from a checkpoint or restart from zero. [[testing-data-pipeline-correctness]] is about catching logic and syntax errors in the transformation code *before* it ever touches production data. [[data-quality-rules-and-anomaly-detection]] is a runtime concern — catching bad data *as it arrives*, whether from a broken rule or a slow-drifting anomaly a fixed threshold would miss. Vu keeps these three explicitly separate rather than treating "testing" as covering all of it, and [[backfilling-data-pipelines]] is where they all have to cooperate at once: a backfill needs source retention (source-side), partition-level granularity (processing-side), regression testing to confirm the new output is meaningfully different where it should be (correctness-side), and resource-pool caps so it doesn't starve other workloads (operational-side).

**The abstract "don't hardcode credentials" principle and its one concrete, worked case study pull in the same direction but at very different altitudes.** [[credentials-and-configuration-management]] states the generic rule — don't hardcode, use an orchestration framework's interface or a cloud secrets manager, version business-logic config alongside code. [[iam-user-vs-role-based-auth-for-pipelines]] is what that rule looks like when actually built: two different AWS mechanisms (long-lived IAM User keys for Airflow, temporary `sts:AssumeRole`-issued credentials for Snowflake) chosen deliberately for two different callers with different constraints, wired through a genuinely awkward two-pass Terraform apply. [[data-access-control-layers]]'s five-tier taxonomy places that same IAM-role mechanism precisely at the "service" layer — verifying which caller is authorized — one layer among infrastructure, table, row, and column controls the credentials note doesn't itself enumerate.

**Two different "quarantine bad things before they spread" patterns sit at two different points in the pipeline.** [[staging-area-raw-to-processed]] is a pre-warehouse validation gate — land data in a raw prefix, clean/validate it into a processed prefix, and only load the processed, validated result into the warehouse, so a partner's malformed file never even reaches dbt. [[dead-letter-queue-and-bad-data-isolation]] is a post-processing isolation pattern — once a record has already been evaluated against business rules and failed, park it somewhere isolated (a DLQ for streams, a dedicated dataset for batch) rather than either silently dropping it or letting it corrupt the good output. Same underlying instinct — don't let bad records travel with good ones — applied at two different pipeline stages.

## Open questions
- Vu is explicit that his sink/source/middle-steps question list "reflects my limited experience... might not cover all the aspects" — the framework itself is stated as provisional, not exhaustive, by its own author.
- [[data-quality-rules-and-anomaly-detection]] names moving averages and "sometimes a machine-learning model" for drift detection but gives no decision rule for when a fixed threshold is enough versus when the added complexity of anomaly detection or ML collaboration is actually justified.
- [[safe-writes-and-schema-evolution-in-serving]] lists five schema-evolution strategies (native table-format evolution, additive-only, versioned tables, schema registry, CI-time tests) but doesn't say how to choose among them for a given situation — only what each buys and costs.
- [[ai-models-as-data-consumers-and-serving-layers]] names "harness engineering" as part of keeping an AI-as-serving-layer consistent, but Vu himself flags that he isn't fully sure he understands the term correctly — a genuine, self-acknowledged gap in the source rather than an omission on the wiki's part.
- [[missing-data-vs-duplicates]] and [[semantic-schema-change]] remain in this topic from an earlier, unsourced snapshot pass. Nothing in this batch's five raw posts confirms or contradicts either claim (the closest material — schema-change notification in [[source-constraints-and-schema-risk]], and drift detection in [[data-quality-rules-and-anomaly-detection]] — is adjacent but doesn't state either concept's specific framing). They are left as-is, not re-grounded, and not deleted.
- [[iam-user-vs-role-based-auth-for-pipelines]]'s resource-pool math (a 90-day backfill queuing up to 90 DAG runs at "25%" resource each, mentioned in [[backfilling-data-pipelines]]) is stated as illustrative — the source doesn't say whether that specific ratio reflects an actual production incident or is a round-number example for the argument.

## Synthesis
Vu Trinh's data pipeline design framework runs sink-first — start from the end users and the business purpose, work out the data model, output shape, and staleness before touching a source or a tool ([[sink-first-requirements-gathering]], [[clarifying-questions-before-tools]]) — then treats the source as the one part you don't control ([[source-constraints-and-schema-risk]]) and the middle as where failure, correctness, and reruns all have to be designed for deliberately rather than assumed. His deeper "system design" pieces reveal that each of those three buckets is far richer than the original question list implied: the sink alone splits into [[batch-vs-stream-throughput-and-latency|throughput-vs-latency infrastructure choices]], [[data-grain-and-serving-storage-shape|grain]], [[physical-layout-partitioning-clustering-and-compaction|physical layout and compaction]], [[concurrency-and-resource-isolation-in-serving|concurrency]], [[safe-writes-and-schema-evolution-in-serving|write safety and schema evolution]], [[data-access-control-layers|access control]], and even [[ai-models-as-data-consumers-and-serving-layers|AI as both consumer and serving layer]] — an axis that didn't exist in the framework's original form at all. The middle steps resolve into distinct, separately named concerns rather than one blob: [[business-rules-and-data-modeling-drift|business-rule drift]], [[data-quality-rules-and-anomaly-detection|data quality checks and anomaly detection]], [[testing-data-pipeline-correctness|pre-deployment testing]], [[pipeline-failure-recovery-and-checkpointing|failure recovery]], [[backfilling-data-pipelines|backfilling]], [[idempotency|idempotent reruns]], [[credentials-and-configuration-management|credential and config management]], and [[processing-layer-observability|observability]] all get their own treatment, with [[dead-letter-queue-and-bad-data-isolation|bad-data isolation]] as the throughline connecting several of them. The guest post's Skytrax rebuild ([[staging-area-raw-to-processed]], [[iam-user-vs-role-based-auth-for-pipelines]], [[snowflake-copy-into-and-event-driven-orchestration]]) is the framework made concrete in Terraform, Airflow, and Snowflake — proof that every abstract question in the sink/source/middle-steps list resolves into a real, specific engineering decision once you actually build the thing. The through-line across all of it, restated at every layer: ask what the data actually needs to do before reaching for a tool to do it.
