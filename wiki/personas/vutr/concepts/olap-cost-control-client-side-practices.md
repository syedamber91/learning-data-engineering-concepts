vutr's starting observation, triggered by a Medium engineering post about cutting Snowflake spend, is that a cloud vendor and its customer actually want the same thing: efficient resource use. Because billing is usage-based, the vendor profits either way, and "no one wants to spend more than the actual value we get" — so the practices that lower your bill are the same ones the vendor's own documentation already encourages. Medium's fixes were deliberately unglamorous: they found pipelines costing thousands a month that supported almost no real use case, columns nobody queried, jobs scheduled more often than needed, partition filters too complex for the pruner to use (so the engine silently fell back to scanning the whole table even though the data *was* partitioned), queries that could tolerate a shorter time window, and queries that could simply tolerate running slower. The fix in every case was equally unglamorous: delete what isn't used, rewrite queries to actually benefit from the existing data layout, adjust schedules, and downsize the warehouse for queries that didn't need the bigger one — enough that Medium ended up spending less than its committed Snowflake credit.

On the storage side, the controllable levers are: store only what you need and let automatic expiration policies clean up the rest; exploit temperature-based tiering (BigQuery's 90-day active-to-long-term offload is the concrete example, at roughly half price); tune the time-travel/history retention window, trading recovery depth for cost; choose a compression scheme deliberately, since the trade is always size-vs-decompression-CPU and "there is no free lunch"; remember that warehouse files are immutable and columnar, so point-level DML is inherently expensive — batch your inserts/updates/deletes; and weigh denormalized (redundant, more storage, faster reads, fewer joins) against normalized (less storage, more joins) layouts, which nested/repeated field support makes easier to navigate either way. Physical-layout techniques — partitioning, clustering, small-file compaction — cut scan cost but are not free: they slow ingestion (writing sorted/clustered/compacted data is slower than blind appends) and add background-task and metadata overhead of their own, so vutr's advice is to use them deliberately, not reflexively.

On the compute side: understand your pricing model *before* trying to optimize it — vutr calls researching these pricing models "one of the most time-consuming processes I've ever experienced" precisely because what triggers a charge is so often non-obvious, and a feature advertised as "free" may only be free on a trial tier. Continuously monitor usage rather than assuming your first resource-size choice was right. Actively limit what's scanned: avoid `SELECT *`, push `WHERE` filters as early as possible, materialize CTEs/views that are referenced repeatedly, and restrict data before a join rather than after. Remember OLAP engines are not built for point look-ups, so batch DML operations rather than issuing them row by row. Use the vendor's own optimization primitives (clustering, partitioning, keys) — but only once you actually understand your data's access pattern, because there genuinely is "no free lunch": vutr notes it's "not rare" to have partitioned a table correctly and still watch a query scan the whole thing anyway, because the filter on the partition column was too complex for the pruner to recognize. Finally, use the vendor's own profiling/observability tooling to find which queries are actually slow or expensive, rather than guessing — and if a query is slow, optimize the query itself before reaching for more compute.

vutr closes his first cost-savings piece with a more speculative reflection rather than a settled claim: quoting Joe Reis's definition of data modeling as "a structured representation that organizes and standardizes data to enable and guide human and machine behavior, inform decision-making, and facilitate actions," he wonders whether cloud warehouses' cheap, elastic resources have caused teams to under-invest in data modeling compared to the on-premises era, when disks were expensive and modelers had to plan carefully in advance. He frames this explicitly as an open question ("I wonder... Would love to hear your thoughts on this"), not a conclusion, and it belongs in this note as a genuine gap rather than a settled practice.
