After [[netflix-streaming-platform-build-and-failure-recovery]]'s crisis-driven Phase 1, Netflix's real-time infrastructure went through three more phases, and the same tension — simplicity versus flexibility — resurfaces at every stage in a slightly different shape.

**Phase 2 (100s of use cases).** Scaling past the MVP surfaced two customer camps: one wanted a simple, fully managed service; the other wanted flexibility and complex computation and was willing to take on infrastructure responsibility (including carrying a pager) to get it. Meanwhile "everything was broken" — Netflix broke S3 once, found numerous Kafka and Flink bugs, hit multiple Titus container-infrastructure failures, and broke Spinnaker (its continuous-deployment platform) as it began programmatically managing hundreds of customer deployments. Netflix's strategic answer was to bias toward the simple side: build a highly abstracted, fully managed service for general streaming use cases, because it covers most data-movement and simple streaming-ETL needs and lets users focus on business logic rather than infrastructure — paired with strict multi-tenancy (one customer's workload must not interfere with another's) and continued DevOps investment so both the platform and its customers could ship changes safely and often. The learning that came out of this phase was as much about prioritization as engineering: deciding what *not* to work on is as important as deciding what to build, and scaling velocity itself needs active balancing — too fast risks tech debt, burnout, and broken trust; too slow leaves customers waiting and teams unmotivated. Netflix also had to correct user misconceptions about stream processing (fears about event loss or correctness under failure) through education and relatable stories.

**Phase 3 (1000s of use cases).** A year after Keystone's launch, internal real-time use cases had grown from under 10 (2015) to a few hundred (2017), and demand shifted toward genuinely custom needs: event-time-based processing, windowing, data-lateness control, state management, richer observability/recovery/troubleshooting tooling, more flexible programming interfaces, data-backfill support, and infrastructure for terabytes of local state. This forced the simplicity-vs-flexibility trade-off into the open: exposing low-level APIs gives users more flexibility but makes the platform harder to operate; abstracting everything away makes operations pleasant but costs flexibility. A second tension — centralization versus localization — appeared alongside it, since other teams already ran their own local platforms on unsupported technology like Spark Streaming and had to be persuaded to move to the centralized stream-processing platform. Netflix's strategy was to build a new Flink-based platform layer underneath both [[keystone-real-time-platform]] and the new custom use cases, deliberately tackling the hardest streaming-ETL and observability problems first since the team was already set up to learn from the most difficult cases. The lesson Netflix draws: simplicity and flexibility aren't opposites, they're stages in one innovation loop — flexibility work done with a few advanced customers proves out new capabilities, which then get simplified for the broader base, which in turn creates new demand for flexibility, restarting the cycle. Treating early, demanding adopters well matters because they become the vocal advocates who help the next round of simplification gain traction.

**Phase 4 (expanding company-wide).** With processing use cases now spread across all of Netflix's organizations, the challenge became ecosystem diversity itself: transactional systems (Cassandra, Postgres, CockroachDB, MySQL) sit alongside analytics systems (Iceberg, Presto, Spark, Druid), and moving data across that technology boundary proved incredibly hard. The growing number of tools created a steep learning curve that slowed adoption and decision-making; machine-learning teams in particular struggled to capitalize on the platform, hit by long feedback loops, reduced data-engineer productivity, and difficulty sharing data with product engineers. And a single centralized point of contact became structurally unsustainable at that scale, pushing Netflix toward local-central platforms — local teams building on top of the shared central platform rather than routing every need through one team. Looking forward, Netflix frames streams as the connective tissue across this diversity — Change Data Capture, streaming materialized views, and Data Mesh-style concepts all being ways to move data across technology boundaries without full centralization — and sees raising the level of abstraction (streaming SQL, blurring the line between streaming and batch) as the path to a more broadly usable platform, alongside deliberate investment in the data quality, scalability, observability, and dev-to-prod feedback loops that machine learning workloads specifically need.
