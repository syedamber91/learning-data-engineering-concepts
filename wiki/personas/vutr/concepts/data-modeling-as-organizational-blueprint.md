Vu treats data modeling as the one skill that must come before every other technical skill, and he repeats his blueprint metaphor identically across three separate articles: "If the data warehouse is a building, data modeling is the blueprint. Without it, we have no clue what to do next." Skip it and you can survive a few months of "blindly loading and querying data," but the nightmare that follows is enumerated the same way each time: maintenance cost (a mess of undocumented SQL scripts and tables), inefficient processing (slow, resource-heavy queries against a poorly designed structure), data integrity problems (no enforced relationships or constraints), weird insights (analysts and data scientists building bad reports on unreliable data), and no trust (business users second-guessing every number). Done well, it instead gives the organization "a common language" for stakeholders and a starting point for data quality and integrity, and it reduces errors because analysts know exactly how to query and engineers know exactly where data lands.

He is explicit that the discipline moves top-down through three layers of increasing technical commitment: the **conceptual model** (business entities like Customers, Products, Orders and their relationships, technology-agnostic, used to align stakeholders), the **logical model** (attributes, primary keys, and data types added to each entity, still technology-agnostic, bridging business concepts to implementation), and the **physical model** (the concrete tables, columns, constraints, and optimizations like clustering or partitioning for a specific system such as BigQuery or Snowflake). For methodology, he consistently recommends the **Kimball / dimensional modeling** approach (star schema: a central fact table of quantitative events surrounded by descriptive dimension tables) as the practical starting point over the **Inmon method** (a top-down, highly normalized 3NF Enterprise Data Warehouse feeding departmental data marts) — Kimball is "easier to get started and practice," while Inmon "excels at large-scale data integration and minimizing data redundancy" but is more complex and less agile. His recommended practice loop: pick a business domain, define the business questions that become fact-table measures, ask how you want to slice those metrics to define dimension tables, build a simple star schema, validate it answers your original questions, and iterate with feedback.

He also directly rebuts two dismissals of data modeling that he says are common. First, that faster, more powerful OLAP systems (Snowflake, BigQuery, Databricks, ClickHouse) have made modeling obsolete — his response is that performance was never the point: modeling's "ultimate goal is to enable and guide human and machine behavior, inform decision-making, and facilitate actions" (a definition he attributes to Joe Reis), a goal that doesn't evaporate just because queries got faster. Second, that a semantic layer sitting on raw data can substitute for modeling entirely — he disagrees: the semantic layer's two processes (declaring assets/relationships/calculations, then letting users consume metrics through friendlier names that compile to SQL) both depend on how the company already stores and organizes its data; skip modeling and "your semantic layer will also be a mess." He extends the same logic into the AI era: "if we want AI to plug in and answer analytics questions, it must first understand the semantics of the data" — modeling is now also a precondition for reliable AI-assisted analytics, not just human ones.

In "the best way to do a data engineering [side project]," this same sequence — problem first, then data modeling, then a tool-agnostic architecture, and only *then* tool selection — is what he holds up as the difference between a candidate who can explain their design decisions and one who, like the college student and his own earlier self, "just searched on the internet for the popular data engineering tools and glued them together" without being able to answer why. That inability to answer "why did you choose those tools" in an interview is the same trap he elsewhere frames as a mistake to move away from, not toward — data modeling is the corrective, not an optional extra step.
