Vu opens this article with a diagnostic story: a college student asked him to review a side project — MySQL → Spark → MinIO/Delta Lake → Trino → Superset — and couldn't answer two questions Vu asked: what (even fake) business question the setup answers, and why he chose that specific stack. Vu admits he did the exact same thing early in his own career ("definitions of done" were just: all stacks are up, some data is transformed, some chart is displayed), put it on his resume, and failed an interview when asked the identical questions. His strategy for a side project that actually helps you get hired is built as a direct corrective, in a specific order.

**Start with a real problem**, ideally your own or one you're genuinely curious about — his example is checking whether his favorite football player is performing well by the numbers — because natural curiosity survives the grind, whereas solving an assigned or random question ("how many Uber rides per day in Singapore") gets boring fast. This mirrors real hiring, since "companies hire data engineers to help them solve real business problems with data," and it also produces a natural answer to "why did you build this" in an interview — a candidate who cares about the problem reads better than one who lists tools. **Then model the data** before touching any tool: define the questions you want answered, derive entities/relationships (conceptual), add attributes/keys/types (logical), then translate to physical tables with optimization techniques (physical) — the same three-layer discipline detailed in [[data-modeling-as-organizational-blueprint]]. He validates a model by checking whether it answers the original questions and whether extending it to new questions is painless, and suggests using AI as a second opinion on the modeling (playing "senior data engineer" or "business user") as long as you give it real context.

**Only then choose tools**, and even then, start with a tool-agnostic architecture (ingestion component, orchestrator, ETL engine, warehouse, BI layer) before naming specific products — he calls resisting the pull toward tool names first "quite hard," since the dopamine of a running Spark job or a dancing Superset chart is real, but the discipline pays off because you can now explain your choices in an interview rather than just listing what you got running. Concrete option lists he gives per component: `dlt` or vanilla Python for API pulls; Airflow, Dagster, or Prefect for orchestration; Iceberg, Delta Lake, or Hudi on S3/GCS for a lakehouse; Spark, Spark Structured Streaming, Flink, or Polars as the ETL engine; Trino, DuckDB, or Spark SQL for querying; BigQuery, Snowflake, Databricks, or DuckDB as the warehouse. Every choice must both solve the actual requirement and have its trade-offs anticipated — "that's how system design looks in the real world."

**Then break things on purpose** — inject a bad record, scale up volume, kill a worker — because each induced failure teaches a category of real production concern: observability (will you know it failed?), fault-tolerance and idempotency (does a retry duplicate data?), resource planning (does the setup scale horizontally, and by how much?), and data quality (what happens with bad records?). **Apply software engineering practices** throughout — Git, CI/CD, tests, clean code, proper secrets handling — explicitly not stored in Google Drive. Finally, **tell the world**: document your design, tech-stack decisions, data model, README, and debugging process, then actually share it (Reddit, LinkedIn, Twitter) with the stated ratio "10% is for bragging... 90% is for getting feedback" (see [[feedback-loop-driven-learning]]). His strongest recommendation here is to make the work tangible rather than a "boring GitHub repo" — expose a live dashboard or build an interactive app (e.g., via Streamlit) so an interviewer can open a browser and see it work, while being careful not to expose sensitive data or credentials in the process.
